# Mesa Brand Guide

---

## 1. Colors

| Role | Name | Hex |
|------|------|-----|
| Primary | Forest | `#1a3a2a` |
| Secondary | Sage | `#52b788` |
| Supporting | Canopy | `#2d6a4f` |
| Background | Cream | `#faf7f2` |
| Text | Charcoal | `#1c1c1e` |

**How to use them:**
- **Forest (#1a3a2a)** — logo background, section headers, anything that anchors the brand
- **Sage (#52b788)** — accent elements, icons, the dot in the logo, highlight color
- **Canopy (#2d6a4f)** — buttons, calls to action, links
- **Cream (#faf7f2)** — page backgrounds, cards, anywhere you need warmth without white
- **Charcoal (#1c1c1e)** — all body text

## 2. Typography

**Headings:** Montserrat Bold
**Body:** Lato Regular

Both are free on Google Fonts and available directly in Google Slides (Insert → Text → Font dropdown). For PowerPoint, download both from fonts.google.com and install before opening the file.

**Why these:** Montserrat's geometric structure matches the clean lines of the logo. Lato reads warm and approachable at small sizes — it doesn't feel clinical, which matters for a health-adjacent product talking to people who are already tired of clinical.

---

## 3. Brand Voice

**Direct. Warm. Practical.**

- **Direct** — Name the real situation. "By Thursday they're soft." "You make rice because the kids will eat it." Customers recognize themselves in specific details, not general wellness language. Never soften the problem.
- **Warm** — This is a hard situation and people feel guilty about it. We're not judging anyone. We're on their side.
- **Practical** — No inspiration without execution. No tips without a plan. Every piece of communication should leave the reader with something they can actually do.

**What to avoid:**
- Wellness jargon ("nourish your body," "fuel your best self")
- Clinical language ("glycemic index," "diabetic-compliant")
- Preachy framing (anything that implies the customer is failing)
- Vague encouragement ("you've got this," "small steps add up")

## 5. Company Description

Mesa is a weekly meal-planning system for families managing health conditions like pre-diabetes on a real budget. Every week, Mesa takes your grocery ceiling, your store's current sales, your family's schedule, and your health needs — and builds a complete, ready-to-use plan before you leave for the store. Not recipes. Not inspiration. A specific list, matched quantities, and meals the whole family will eat. Mesa exists because the problem was never a lack of information or good intentions — it was the absence of a system that connects all four constraints at once. We built the coordination layer that was missing.
