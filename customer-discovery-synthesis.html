Section 01

Venture Overview

Refined Problem Statement

Pre-diabetic adults managing work, school, and family caregiving have enough awareness that certain foods worsen their condition but no practical system to act on that awareness consistently. Every week they buy produce with good intentions that spoils before it is used, shop without a list, spend more than they planned, and default to meals the family will accept rather than meals safe for their blood sugar. The barrier is not motivation — every interviewee demonstrated intent. It is the absence of a system that connects grocery budget, weekly schedule, family food preferences, and blood sugar logic into something repeatable that does not require starting from scratch each week.

What changed from the original framing: The original problem statement claimed “the problem is not a lack of nutritional knowledge.” The interviews do not support that claim. Mary did not know whether oatmeal was safe for her blood sugar. Grace knew ramen was bad and bought bread and rice on the same trip without recognizing the same glycemic problem. A knowledge gap is real and coexists with the system gap. The refined statement acknowledges both without making the product responsible for solving both.

Target Customer

A pre-diabetic adult, 35 or older, who has been told by a doctor they are pre-diabetic or at high risk of Type 2 diabetes. They work full time, are a primary caregiver for a family, and operate on a grocery budget tight enough that $60 is a ceiling, not a preference. They know one or two foods that affect blood sugar — rice, ramen, “carbs” — but cannot apply that logic consistently. They have tried at least one structured resource and stopped using it. They are not looking for more information. They are looking for something that works on a Tuesday night when they are exhausted.

Evidence base: Tere, Grace, and Mary from the five interviews. Nelsy (high blood pressure, not pre-diabetic) and Veronica (no blood sugar framework, dislikes vegetables) are excluded from the primary profile. The evidence base is three interviews — directionally consistent, not statistically validated.

Competitive Landscape

Competitor What they built Why they fail this customer
PlateJoy
2013–2025 • $12.3M raised		 Personalized meal planning + CDC-recognized Diabetes Prevention Program for pre-diabetics with virtual coaching. Pivoted to B2B insurance, making revenue dependent on user health outcomes it could not control. HIPAA restricted communication. Acquired 2021, shut down 2025.
Klinio Diabetes-labeled wellness app. Targets clinical/insurance channel. Does not integrate budget, perishability, or schedule.
eMeals & general apps Generic meal planning with diabetes-friendly tags. Too rigid, not built for pre-diabetic constraints or bulk grocery reality. $5–15/month is a structural barrier before grocery costs.
Meal kits Pre-portioned ingredients delivered weekly. Too expensive. Nelsy canceled after two weeks on price alone.
The gap: No product integrates all four constraints simultaneously — pre-diabetes blood sugar logic, a real grocery budget, ingredient perishability, and a family’s weekly schedule — into a repeatable, automated system. Every competitor solves one or two. None connect all four. None account for the mismatch between how food is sold (two-pound bags) and how meals are planned (one recipe).

Direction

Leaning toward direct-to-consumer AI-powered weekly food system (Remix 1): Takes a family’s budget, refrigerator inventory, local grocery sales, and weekly schedule and generates a fresh, blood-sugar-safe meal plan every week. Two core leverage points:

  • Ingredient-first planning: Plan three meals around one bag of green beans before buying it. Breaks the waste loop at the source.
  • Built-in variety engine: Same ingredient, different preparation each day. Breaks the fatigue loop and prevents abandonment.

B2B institutional (Remix 2) and grocery chain partnership (Remix 3) remain viable as future business model layers once the core product is validated. The “why now” argument: AI can now reason across budget, perishability, schedule, and blood sugar constraints simultaneously in a way that was not practical before.

Interview Synthesis — Five Profiles

All quotes are exact. Observations below each quote set are drawn from in-transcript annotations. Challenges to assumptions are flagged separately.

Interview 1 — Tere

Pre-diabetic • Working adult • Family caregiver
  • “Honestly, I didn’t really ‘decide.’ I ate the same thing I usually eat — hard-boiled egg and a couple of quinoa crackers and coffee in the morning, skipped lunch because work was crazy, and then made rice, beans, and chicken for dinner because it’s what the kids will eat. I know the rice isn’t great for my sugar, but I was too tired to think of something else.”
  • “All the time. I buy vegetables with good intentions, but by the time I get around to cooking, they’re already soft or moldy. I feel guilty throwing them away, but it happens every week.”
  • “I went after work, already exhausted. I didn’t have a list. I just walked around grabbing things I usually buy. I looked at some healthier stuff, but it felt expensive, and I didn’t know what meals to make with it, so I put it back.”
  • “Not really. I usually just watch YouTube recipes, but they use ingredients I don’t have, and TikTok videos because the videos are shorter.”
  • “Honestly? I just don’t eat or I repeat the same meal again. I have the intention, but not a good system.”
Challenges my assumptions She knows rice worsens her blood sugar and eats it every night — not from ignorance but because it is the only thing the kids will eat. The unit of analysis is the household, not the individual. A blood-sugar-safe plan that the children will not eat does not get followed. Produce waste produces guilt, not just financial frustration — she describes it as inevitable, not as a solvable problem. The product pitch may need to address the emotional dimension, not just the logistical one.

Interview 2 — Mary

Pre-diabetic • Working adult • Family caregiver
  • “I had oatmeal for breakfast because it’s ‘healthy,’ even though I’m not sure about the sugar. Lunch was fast food because I was running errands. Dinner was whatever I could make quickly — Mac and Cheese and a chicken from Publix.”
  • “Yes. Especially spinach and berries. They go bad so fast. I buy them because I know I should eat healthier, but I never plan meals around them.”
  • “I went on Sunday. I tried to buy healthier things, but I didn’t know what meals to make. I ended up buying the same stuff I always buy. I spent more than I wanted to.”
  • “I tried a low carb cookbook, but the recipes were too complicated. I don’t have time for all that prep.”
  • “TikTok. But those recipes look good and then I never make them. I think I have all the ingredients and then I’m always missing a couple of them.”
Challenges my assumptions Mary directly contradicts the original claim that “the problem is not a lack of nutritional knowledge.” She does not know whether oatmeal is safe for her blood sugar. She ate Mac and Cheese for dinner with no apparent blood sugar flag — same glycemic problem as rice, not recognized. She also overspends even when not buying healthier food, suggesting the budget problem is driven by the absence of a plan, not the cost of healthy ingredients.

Interview 3 — Grace

Pre-diabetic / high risk • Budget-constrained
  • “I ate whatever was cheap. Eggs for breakfast, ramen for lunch, and a frozen meal for dinner. I know ramen is bad for sugar, but it’s cheap and fast — and then I try to go walking… hahah”
  • “Yes. I buy veggies thinking I’ll cook, but then I’m too tired after work. They sit in the fridge until they’re gross, or I cook them all at once to eat over a couple of days and they get soggy.”
  • “I went with a strict budget — $60. I bought basics: eggs, bread, chicken, rice. I wanted to buy healthier stuff, but it didn’t fit the budget. I did buy mixed frozen vegetables.”
  • “I tried a diabetes app once, but it felt like it was made for older people. Not helpful.”
  • “Google. But it gives too many answers and I don’t know which one to look at. I feel like I spend a lot of time.”
Challenges my assumptions Grace knows ramen is bad for blood sugar and on the same trip bought bread and rice without flagging the same glycemic problem. The knowledge does not generalize across similar foods. She also tried batch cooking as a self-developed workaround — and it failed on texture. The product needs to account for palatability across multiple days, not just planning. Her $60 budget is a hard ceiling: any system that increases the upfront grocery bill fails regardless of long-term savings.

Interview 4 — Nelsy

High blood pressure • NOT pre-diabetic • No professional support
Note: Nelsy is not pre-diabetic. Her dietary constraint is low sodium, not blood sugar management. Her data validates the structural failure pattern but does not confirm blood sugar logic. One of five interviewees is outside the target diagnosis.
  • “I try to avoid salt, but it’s hard. I ate cereal for breakfast, a sandwich for lunch, and baked chicken for dinner. I decide based on what’s fast.”
  • “Yes. Especially things like zucchini or herbs. I buy them for one recipe and then don’t use the rest.”
  • “I walked around and bought the main things we always eat. I know I need low sodium options, but I don’t know which ones. I read labels, but it takes forever.”
  • “I tried a meal kit once, but it was too expensive. I canceled after two weeks.”
  • “I call mom and she gives me these recipes. They are healthy, but I don’t end up cooking them.”
Challenges my assumptions Nelsy receives trusted, personalized, healthy guidance from her mother and does not act on it. The source is trusted. The recipes are good. The advice is personalized. She still does not cook them. This is the clearest evidence in the data set that the barrier is not trust, not information quality, not source credibility — it is the gap between receiving a recommendation and completing the execution work. Trust alone does not drive behavior.

Interview 5 — Veronica

Pre-diabetic / high risk
  • “I had yogurt for breakfast, skipped lunch, and ordered takeout for dinner. I didn’t plan anything. I just went with whatever was easiest.”
  • “Yes. I buy fruit and veggies, but I forget they’re there. I don’t know how to use them before they spoil. I don’t like vegetables. I know I need to eat them though.”
  • “I walked in with an idea of what I should buy, and I bought things that ‘seemed healthy,’ but I didn’t know what meals to make. I spent too much.”
  • “I tried a free meal prep app, but it felt like homework. Too many notifications.”
  • “Pinterest. But I never actually cook the recipes.”
Challenges my assumptions Veronica does not like vegetables — the only interviewee to say so directly. A planning system cannot fix palatability. She is also the strongest evidence against a price-first adoption strategy: she tried a free app and abandoned it anyway because of friction. Price is not the only barrier. Engagement mechanics that demand ongoing input create abandonment at zero cost. She is excluded from the primary target profile on two grounds: no blood sugar framework and a palatability barrier the product cannot address.

Every interviewee considered buying healthier food and did not, because they could not connect the item to a specific meal. Every structured resource was tried and abandoned

Five different tools. Five different failure reasons: ingredient mismatch (Tere), complexity (Mary), demographic irrelevance (Grace), price (Nelsy), friction (Veronica). The variety of failure modes matters — the problem is not fixable by improving any one dimension of an existing tool.

Confirmed — 5 of 5

Speed and ease override health when there is no plan

When the plan breaks down — which it does regularly — every interviewee defaults to what is fastest, not healthiest. This is a systems finding, not a motivation finding. The fix is a system that removes the decision point, not a nudge that reminds them to decide better.

“I decide based on what’s fast.” — Nelsy
“I just went with whatever was easiest.” — Veronica
Confirmed — 3 of 5

Overspending despite not buying healthier food

Mary, Veronica, and Tere (implied) all spent more than intended without filling the cart with healthy food. Without a plan, the cart fills with familiar defaults, overspending happens anyway, and the healthier items do not make it in. The system saves money not by being cheap but by making the existing budget do what it was intended to do.

“I ended up buying the same stuff I always buy. I spent more than I wanted to.” — Mary

Contradictions

Contradiction — Challenges the venture directly

They know a specific food is bad for them and eat it every day

Tere knows rice worsens her blood sugar and eats it every night. Grace knows ramen is bad and eats it for lunch. Knowledge is present. Behavior does not change. The product’s answer must be that the system removes the decision entirely — the better option is prepped and accessible before the exhaustion point arrives. But that mechanism depends on the customer following the plan in advance, which is the behavior the venture is trying to produce. The circularity is real and unresolved.

“I know the rice isn’t great for my sugar, but I was too tired to think of something else.” — Tere
“I know ramen is bad for sugar, but it’s cheap and fast.” — Grace
Contradiction — Complicates the problem statement

Grace’s blood sugar knowledge does not transfer across similar foods

Grace knows ramen is bad for blood sugar. On the same trip she bought bread and rice with no apparent flag — the same glycemic problem, unrecognized. The knowledge does not generalize. A system that surfaces brief rationale cannot assume the customer will connect “bread is like ramen” without being told explicitly.

Contradiction — Reframes the trust problem

Trusted, personalized guidance from a human being does not produce action

Nelsy’s mother gives her healthy, personalized recipes from a completely trusted source. She does not cook them. The failure is not in the source, quality, or credibility of the advice. It is in the gap between receiving a recommendation and completing the execution work. This reframes the AI trust question: “will she trust an AI?” may be the wrong question. The right question is whether the output is complete enough that she does not have to do anything after receiving it except follow it.

“I call mom and she gives me these recipes. They are healthy, but I don’t end up cooking them.” — Nelsy
Contradiction — Against a price-first strategy

A free product was abandoned for reasons unrelated to price

Veronica tried a free dedicated app and abandoned it because it felt like homework and had too many notifications. Price is a structural barrier confirmed by the competitive analysis and three interviews. But removing price entirely does not solve adoption. Product design — low friction, pull-based, complete output without ongoing engagement demands — is an equal or greater barrier.

“I tried a free meal prep app, but it felt like homework. Too many notifications.” — Veronica

Surprises

Surprise

Produce waste carries guilt, not just financial frustration

The original framing positioned waste as a financial and logistical failure. Tere’s response revealed an emotional dimension: she feels guilty — she is measuring herself against a standard she believes in and failing to meet it weekly. “Stop feeling guilty every week” and “save money on groceries” are different value propositions aimed at different emotional drivers. The guilt framing may be more motivating for this customer.

Surprise

No interviewee mentioned a doctor or healthcare provider as a food resource

All five are pre-diabetic or high risk. Not one mentioned their doctor, a nurse, a dietitian referral, or any healthcare provider in response to where they go for food help. Sources named: TikTok, YouTube, Pinterest, Google, a cookbook, a diabetes app, a meal kit, a mother. Healthcare providers are absent from the entire data set. The HCP referral channel is a logical opportunity but has zero direct support from these interviews.

Surprise

Grace is already trying to solve the problem — and her solution created a new problem

Grace developed a workaround (batch cooking all vegetables at once) and found it failed on texture by day three. She is actively problem-solving and hitting the limits of what an individual can solve without a real system. The product must account for palatability across multiple days, not just planning. Ingredient-first planning solves the buying problem; it does not automatically solve what happens when broccoli is soggy on Thursday.

“I cook them all at once to eat over a couple of days and they get soggy.” — Grace

Section 06

Remaining Risks and Next Steps

Risk 1 — Primary: AI trust for a medically sensitive recommendation (untested)

No interviewee was asked about AI. The entire product depends on this. What the interviews do show is that trust in digital tools generally is low: every tool tried was abandoned, and the most trusted source named across all five interviews was a person (Nelsy’s mother). Whether AI changes the trust calculus — because the plan is personalized — or makes it worse — because it is opaque and making health recommendations — is unknown.

Validation question: “If something built your weekly grocery plan around your actual budget, your schedule, and what’s safe for your blood sugar — and it got it right — would it matter to you whether a person or an AI built it? What would change about your answer?”

Risk 2: Willingness to pay is completely unvalidated

Not one of the five interviewees was asked what they would pay. Budget constraints are confirmed. A specific price point is not. The revenue model cannot be chosen with confidence until this is tested directly.

Risk 3: Behavior change before the crisis point

The system removes the decision only if the customer follows the plan in advance. Tere has to shop on Sunday, buy what the plan says, and have better food in the house before Tuesday night arrives. That is not zero behavior change — it is behavior change shifted earlier in the week. The interviews confirm intent. They do not confirm that any of these five people will build a new habit around a weekly planning tool when they have already abandoned every structured resource they have tried.

Risk 4: Liability from incorrect glycemic guidance

The product generates food recommendations for people managing a diagnosed medical condition. A wrong recommendation has medical consequences. Clinical validation of the glycemic logic library and legal review of the output format and disclaimers are non-optional before launch. Neither has been scoped or costed in any document.
