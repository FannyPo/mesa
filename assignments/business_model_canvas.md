## Block 1: Customer Segments

### Primary Segment: The Pre-Diabetic Family Manager
A pre-diabetic adult, 35 or older, who has received a pre-diabetes diagnosis or has been told they are at high risk of Type 2 diabetes progression. They work full time, serve as primary caregiver for a family, and operate on a grocery budget tight enough that it functions as a ceiling, not a preference. They have partial blood sugar awareness — they can name one or two foods that affect their condition — but cannot apply that logic consistently across unfamiliar situations or their own weekly habits. They have tried at least one structured resource (an app, a cookbook, a meal kit, social media content) and abandoned it.

**Evidence base:** Tere, Grace, and Mary from the five discovery interviews. All three are confirmed pre-diabetic, all three demonstrated the same structural failure pattern (weekly produce waste, unplanned grocery trips, tool abandonment, inspiration that does not convert to meals). All three have partial blood sugar awareness — they know one or two bad foods — but cannot apply the logic consistently.

**Who is excluded and why:**
- Nelsy is not pre-diabetic. Her condition is high blood pressure and her dietary framework is sodium. Her structural frustrations (waste, label reading burden, price sensitivity) overlap with this segment, but her product needs are different. Including her blurs the blood sugar logic requirement at the center of this venture.
- Veronica has no blood sugar framework and stated directly that she does not like vegetables. She is pre-diabetic or high risk but represents a different behavioral starting point — one the product cannot address without first solving a palatability and motivation problem that is out of scope.

**The unresolved payer problem:**
This customer cannot reliably afford a $5–15/month subscription before adding grocery costs. Three of five interviewees surfaced cost as a direct constraint. Nelsy canceled a meal kit specifically because of price. Grace operates on a $60 grocery ceiling. The competitive analysis confirmed this pricing range is a structural barrier, not an inconvenience.

This creates a tension the canvas has not resolved: the pre-diabetic family manager is the *user* — the person who receives and needs the value — but may not be the *customer* — the person who pays for it. If affordability cannot be solved at the consumer level, the paying customer may be a community health clinic, an employer benefits program, or a regional grocery chain (see Remix 2 and Remix 3 in the competitive analysis). That determination changes the sales model, the product design priorities, and the partnership strategy. It must be resolved before the Revenue Streams and Channels blocks are filled in.

**Size signal:** The CDC estimates 96–98 million American adults have pre-diabetes. The American Diabetes Association estimates 10–20% actively attempt dietary management at any given time — roughly 10–20 million adults. The subset that is simultaneously working, budget-constrained, and managing a household represents the sharpest entry point. No validated sizing for that sub-segment exists in this research.

## Block 2: Value Propositions

### Core Value Proposition
A complete weekly food system that does the coordination work the target customer cannot do alone — connecting grocery budget, ingredient perishability, family schedule, and blood sugar safety into a plan she can follow on a Tuesday night when she is exhausted, without starting from scratch every week.

**Why this framing and not "blood-sugar-safe meal planning":**
"Blood-sugar-safe meal planning" is a feature. The value the interviews actually describe is an outcome: food that gets eaten instead of thrown away, a grocery budget that goes where it was meant to go, and blood sugar logic handled in the background rather than requiring manual reasoning every time a food decision is made. Tere did not say she needed a nutrition system. She said she had the intention and not a good system. The value proposition meets her where she is.

### Value delivered, by evidence source

**1. Eliminates weekly produce waste**
Confirmed across all five interviews without exception. All five buy produce with good intentions that spoils before it is used. Tere described weekly guilt about throwing food away. Grace identified texture failure from batch cooking as a separate failure mode — vegetables cooked all at once become soggy by day three. The ingredient-first planning mechanism (plan three meals around one bag of green beans before buying it, not after) directly attacks this. It breaks the waste loop at the source rather than at the symptom.
*Evidence: Tere — "I feel guilty throwing them away, but it happens every week." Grace — "They sit in the fridge until they're gross, or I cook them all at once to eat over a couple of days and they get soggy."*

**2. Converts inspiration to execution**
The most consistent single finding across all five interviews: social media, recipes, and content all fail to bridge to an actual meal. The problem is not lack of ideas — it is the chain from idea to execution breaking at the ingredient mismatch step. Mary thinks she has the ingredients and is always missing a couple. Tere watches YouTube recipes that use ingredients she does not have. Veronica saves Pinterest recipes she never makes. The value is not more recipes or content. It is a plan built around what she can actually buy and already has.
*Evidence: Mary — "Those recipes look good and then I never make them. I think I have all the ingredients and then I'm always missing a couple of them." Tere — "They use ingredients I don't have."*

**3. Removes the weekly restart**
Every existing approach requires starting from scratch each week. The five why analysis identified this as the structural failure underneath fatigue and abandonment: no system carries forward, so every week begins with the same cognitive load and the same default outcomes. A weekly system with built-in variety — same ingredient, different preparation across the week — removes both the restart burden and the dietary fatigue that drives abandonment.
*Evidence: Tere — "I have the intention, but not a good system." Confirmed structurally in the systems analysis.*

**4. Handles blood sugar logic without requiring complete nutritional knowledge**
The target customer has partial blood sugar awareness — enough to know a few bad foods, not enough to apply the logic consistently or to evaluate unfamiliar situations. The system does the blood sugar reasoning in the background. Recommendations surface brief rationale so the customer can spot-check them against what she already believes, without requiring a nutrition course.
*Evidence: Mary — "I'm not sure about the sugar" in oatmeal. Grace — knows ramen is bad, bought bread and rice on the same trip with no flag. The value is handling what she cannot handle consistently.*

**5. Works for the whole household, not just the person managing blood sugar**
This was not in the original framing and emerged directly from the interviews. Tere eats rice every night because the kids will eat it. A blood-sugar-safe plan that the family will not eat gets abandoned immediately. The system has to account for household food preferences alongside individual dietary constraints. This is not a convenience feature — it is a retention requirement.
*Evidence: Tere — "I made rice, beans, and chicken for dinner because it's what the kids will eat. I know the rice isn't great for my sugar, but I was too tired to think of something else."*

### What the value proposition does not claim
- It does not claim to be a medical device or to replace a dietitian. The liability question from the assumption scorecard (wrong glycemic guidance creates medical risk) is unresolved. The value is practical food planning, not clinical nutrition management.
- It does not claim the customer will trust an AI system for food decisions. That assumption is inconclusive — untested in all five interviews. Trust is a prerequisite for this value being delivered, and it has not been validated.

## Block 3: Channels

### How the target customer currently finds solutions — and where that fails

Every interviewee named at least one platform they use when they need food help:
- TikTok: Tere, Mary
- YouTube: Tere
- Pinterest: Veronica
- Google: Grace
- A diabetes app: Grace
- A low-carb cookbook: Mary
- A meal kit: Nelsy
- A family member (mom): Nelsy

These people are reachable. The problem is not invisibility — it is that every channel they already use fails at the same point. The content inspires but does not execute. Tere watches YouTube recipes that use ingredients she does not have. Mary finds TikTok recipes and is always missing a couple of ingredients. Veronica saves Pinterest recipes she never makes. Grace searches Google and is paralyzed by too many answers.

The channel insight from the interviews is not "find a new way to reach them." It is: **they are already consuming food content on platforms they use daily; the failure happens at the last mile between inspiration and an actual plan.** The channel strategy should intercept that gap rather than build a new awareness path from scratch.

### Channels that are ruled out by the evidence

**App store download:** Veronica tried a free dedicated app and found it felt "like homework" with "too many notifications." Grace tried a diabetes app and found it "felt like it was made for older people." App fatigue is confirmed in this customer. A standalone app requiring download, account creation, and ongoing engagement is the format that has already been tried and abandoned most consistently. Asking this customer to download something new is asking her to repeat the failure she has already experienced.

**Email-based delivery:** No interviewee mentioned email as a source of food help or as a trusted channel for any kind of daily guidance. No evidence supports it.

**Healthcare provider referral as primary channel:** No interviewee mentioned their doctor as a source of food or meal guidance, despite all being pre-diabetic or high risk. This may be a viable secondary channel for credibility or institutional distribution (Remix 2), but the interviews give no evidence it drives direct consumer behavior.

### Channels supported by the evidence

**Short-form social content on TikTok and YouTube (awareness channel)**
Three of five interviewees actively use TikTok or YouTube for food content. This is where the target customer already goes when she wants food help. Organic or paid content that demonstrates the specific failure she experiences — produce wasting, missing two ingredients, the plan falling apart by Tuesday — and shows the system closing that gap meets her where she already is, without requiring a new behavior.
*Evidence: Tere — "TikTok videos because the videos are shorter." Mary — "TikTok. But those recipes look good and then I never make them."*

**SMS or WhatsApp (delivery channel)**
Neither requires a download. Both are already open daily. A weekly plan delivered by text — groceries listed, quantities matched to what she is buying, meals sequenced by perishability — arrives in a channel she already checks without creating a new engagement obligation. The interaction to set up the plan (budget, schedule, family preferences) happens once at onboarding and updates only when something changes.
This is not validated by the interviews directly — no interviewee was asked about SMS or WhatsApp delivery. It is an inference from the low-friction delivery constraint and the evidence that app fatigue is real. It should be tested.

**A grocery chain's existing app (delivery and discovery channel — Remix 3)**
The most structurally sound channel answer, because it puts the system inside an app the target customer already opens weekly for a directly related task. Grace shops with a $60 hard budget. Tere shops after work, exhausted, without a list. Both of them are already in a grocery context where a plan built around that week's sales circular and their budget would be immediately useful. The grocery chain funds access; the customer pays nothing and downloads nothing new.
This channel is the cleanest solution to both the low-friction constraint and the unresolved payer problem from Block 1. Its risk is the one named in the competitive analysis: grocery chains move slowly, and a pilot deal could exceed the venture timeline.

**Community health clinic or employer referral (institutional channel — Remix 2)**
Not supported by the interview evidence directly — no interviewee mentioned a clinic or employer as a source of food guidance. Supported structurally: community health clinics serving pre-diabetic patients and employers with at-risk employee populations have financial incentive to prevent diabetes progression. This channel reaches the target customer through a trusted, credible source without requiring her to find the product herself. Its risk is sales cycle length: a single institutional deal could take six to twelve months to close.

### The channel tension that is not resolved

The low-friction constraint and the personalization requirement are in direct conflict. Personalization — budget, schedule, family preferences, fridge inventory — requires input. Every input is friction. The question is not how to eliminate friction but how to front-load it once at setup and then deliver value without asking for more. No channel tested in these five interviews solves that fully. SMS and the grocery app come closest. Both need to be tested directly with the target customer before this block is treated as settled.

## Block 4: Customer Relationships

### What kind of relationship the interviews describe

The interviewees did not describe wanting a product relationship. They described wanting a person who already knows their situation and hands them what they need.

Nelsy calls her mother for recipes. The advice is good, the source is trusted, it arrives without demands or notifications. That relationship still does not produce action — but it is the closest any interviewee came to describing what they actually want. The failure is not the relationship model; it is that even trusted, personalized advice does not bridge to execution without a ready-to-use output attached to it.

Tere wants guidance but "not something time-consuming." Veronica found a free app felt like homework because of notifications — the app demanded her ongoing attention and she did not want to give it. Mary abandoned a cookbook because the recipes were too complicated to fit into her life. Grace is paralyzed by Google because it gives too many answers and requires her to filter them. Every failed relationship in the interview set asked the customer to do more work. The relationship that works asks her to do less.

### What this means for the product

**Pull-based, not push-based.** The customer comes to the system when she needs it — Sunday evening before the week starts — and the system delivers a complete output without requiring her to manage an ongoing relationship. It does not send daily reminders. It does not ask her to log meals. It does not require her to track her blood sugar inside the product. She gets a weekly plan and a grocery list. That is the output. The relationship ends there until next week.
*Evidence: Veronica — "It felt like homework. Too many notifications." Tere — "She wants guidance but not something time-consuming."*

**Trusted, not clinical.** Grace found a diabetes app that felt designed for older people and did not use it. The relationship the product establishes cannot feel like a medical intervention or a chronic disease management program. It has to feel like practical, relevant help from something that understands her life — budget, schedule, family, the Tuesday night exhaustion — not a clinical protocol that happens to touch food.
*Evidence: Grace — "I tried a diabetes app once, but it felt like it was made for older people. Not helpful."*

**Delivers a complete output, not guidance to act on.** Nelsy receives healthy recipes from her mother and does not cook them. The gap is not the quality of the advice — it is that the advice requires her to convert it into a plan, a grocery list, and a set of decisions she does not have the time or system to make. The relationship delivers a ready-to-execute output, not information she has to act on herself. The value arrives complete.
*Evidence: Nelsy — "I call mom and she gives me these recipes. They are healthy, but I don't end up cooking them."*

**Familiarity over novelty.** The product should not feel new. It should feel like the next version of something she already does — a grocery list she already makes, a weekly routine she already has, a channel she already uses — made better. Framing it as a new tool to learn is the framing that produces abandonment. Framing it as the missing piece in what she already does is the framing the interviews support.

### The AI trust problem in this block

The relationship model above assumes the customer will accept recommendations from an AI system for a medically sensitive condition. That assumption is inconclusive — it was never tested in any of the five interviews. What the interviews do show is that trust in digital tools generally is low: every tool tried was abandoned, and the most trusted source named across all five interviews is a person (Nelsy's mother), not a platform.

Whether AI changes the trust calculus — because the plan is personalized to her specific budget, schedule, and family rather than generic — or makes it worse — because it is an algorithm making health recommendations — is unknown. This is the highest-priority question for the next interview round and cannot be answered from the current evidence.

**Suggested test question for next interviews:** "If something built your weekly grocery plan around your actual budget, your schedule, and what's safe for your blood sugar — and it got it right — would it matter to you whether a person or an AI built it? What would change about your answer?"

## Block 5: Revenue Streams

### What the interviews actually said about price

Almost nothing directly useful. No interviewee was asked what they would pay for a food planning system. That question does not appear in any of the five transcripts. Every pricing signal in this block is inference from behavioral evidence, not stated willingness to pay.

What the interviews did surface:
- Nelsy canceled a meal kit for being too expensive. Meal kits run $60–120/week or $8–15/meal. This establishes an outer ceiling she will not cross, not a threshold she will accept.
- Veronica tried a free dedicated app and abandoned it. This is the most important pricing signal in the data set: **price alone does not determine adoption**. Even at zero dollars, the wrong product gets abandoned. The value must be unmistakably clear on the first use, regardless of cost.
- Grace operates on a $60 hard grocery budget. This is a ceiling on weekly spend, not a statement about a monthly subscription fee — but it means any system that increases her upfront grocery cost fails before the subscription price is even considered.
- Mary overspent on groceries without a plan. She is already spending more than she wants to — a system that demonstrably reduces that waste has a financial value proposition she could articulate. But she was not asked to put a number on it.

The competitive analysis established that $5–15/month is a structural barrier for this customer segment before grocery costs are added. That finding came from competitive research, not from the interviews themselves.

### The three revenue models and their tradeoffs

**Option 1: Direct-to-consumer subscription**
The pre-diabetic adult pays a monthly fee directly.

Revenue logic: Clean model, shortest path to revenue, full control over the product experience and customer relationship. No intermediary, no long sales cycle.

Problem: The customer segment confirmed across three interviews is structurally price-sensitive. Subscription pricing at any level that sustains a software business requires a customer willing and able to pay monthly. Grace's $60 grocery ceiling and Nelsy's abandoned meal kit suggest this customer prioritizes her grocery budget over a planning tool. If the product cannot demonstrate immediate, tangible savings — less wasted food, less overspending — the subscription fee competes with grocery dollars.

Floor price that makes the model viable: unknown. No interviewee was asked. This must be tested before this option can be evaluated seriously.

**Option 2: Institutional B2B — clinic or employer pays**
A community health clinic, FQHC, or employer benefits program funds free access for pre-diabetic patients or employees. The pre-diabetic family manager uses the product free of charge.

Revenue logic: The financial case for an institutional payer is strong. A single patient progressing from pre-diabetes to Type 2 diabetes costs an estimated $9,600–$16,000 per year in treatment. A prevention tool at $30–50 per member per month has an asymmetric ROI for any payer managing downstream diabetes costs. PlateJoy validated that institutional payers will engage with this category — it raised $12.3M partly on this logic.

PlateJoy's failure in this model was specific: it routed through insurance reimbursement, which added HIPAA constraints on communication and tied revenue to behavior outcomes it could not control. A direct contract with a clinic or employer, without insurance intermediation, avoids both failure modes.

Problem: Sales cycles are 6–12 months minimum for institutional deals. This model has zero interview validation — no interviewee mentioned a clinic or employer as a source of food guidance or health support. The financial logic is sound. The pathway to a first contract is unproven.

**Option 3: Grocery chain partnership**
A regional grocery chain funds free or subsidized access for shoppers. The system is built around the chain's weekly sales circular and local inventory. The chain captures a loyal, intentional shopper making predictable weekly purchases. The user pays nothing and downloads nothing new.

Revenue logic: The grocery chain's incentive is direct — basket size lift and shopper loyalty. A pre-diabetic shopper whose weekly plan is built around the store's sales produces a predictable, higher-value weekly basket than a shopper without a plan. The licensing or partnership fee comes from the chain, not the consumer.

This model solves the payer problem and the channel problem simultaneously. It is the structurally cleanest option for reaching this customer.

Problem: Grocery chains move slowly. Finding and closing a pilot partnership requires an internal champion, procurement cycles, and a proof of concept. None of that is fast. The competitive analysis flagged this risk explicitly: a pilot deal could exceed the venture timeline.

### Who captures the most value and who is most likely to pay

The entity with the greatest financial upside from preventing diabetes progression is an institutional payer — an employer or health plan managing a population with high pre-diabetes prevalence. The ROI math is strongly in their favor. They are also the slowest to move and the hardest to access without an existing relationship.

The entity most likely to pay quickly is the direct consumer — but the evidence says she cannot reliably pay enough to make unit economics work at standard software margins.

The grocery chain sits between: meaningful financial incentive, faster to engage than a health plan, and the partnership model sidesteps the consumer price sensitivity problem. It is the most structurally attractive option and the least evidentially validated.

### Recommended sequencing — not validated, stated as a hypothesis

Use direct-to-consumer (very low price or free with a clear upgrade path) to validate that the product works and that users follow the plan. Use that evidence — retention rate, plan follow-through, measurable waste reduction — to sell the institutional or grocery channel. The consumer validates the product. The institution or chain provides the sustainable revenue.

This sequencing is not supported by interview evidence. It is a logical inference from the evidence that does exist. It should be tested.

## Block 6: Cost Structure

### What can be stated from current evidence

The cost structure cannot be fully specified at this stage. The revenue model is unresolved, and cost structure follows revenue model: a direct-to-consumer product has different cost drivers than a B2B institutional contract or a grocery chain integration. What can be identified are the cost categories that will be material in any version of this venture, and the one cost category that carries risk beyond normal software operating costs.

**Technology and AI infrastructure**
The core product runs on AI inference — generating personalized weekly plans across budget, perishability, schedule, and blood sugar constraints in real time. Inference costs scale with usage. At low volume during validation, this cost is negligible. At scale, it is the primary variable cost in the model. Hosting, data storage, and integration costs (grocery chain sales circular data, if pursued) add to this category.

**Blood sugar logic validation**
The assumption scorecard identified incorrect glycemic guidance as a liability risk. Someone with clinical or nutritional expertise needs to validate that the system's blood sugar recommendations are accurate before the product is used by anyone managing a real medical condition. This cost does not appear in the current documents and has not been scoped. It is not optional.

**Customer acquisition**
Cost structure here depends entirely on which channel is primary. Direct-to-consumer through TikTok and YouTube content requires content production, paid distribution, or both. B2B institutional requires a sales function. Grocery chain partnership requires business development capacity. These are structurally different cost lines with different payback periods.

**Legal and liability exposure**
This venture sits in a space adjacent to medical guidance. The product does not claim to be a medical device, but it generates food recommendations for people managing a diagnosed condition. If a recommendation is wrong and causes a blood sugar event, the exposure is real. The liability cost is unknown in dollar terms but is not zero. Insurance, legal review of outputs, and disclaimer language all carry cost. This has not been addressed anywhere in the current documents. It needs to be.

**What is deliberately excluded from this block**
Headcount, infrastructure specifics, and margin targets are not included here. The evidence base does not support specific figures, and including fabricated numbers would misrepresent what is known. These will need to be built from actual operational decisions once the revenue model is chosen.

## Block 7: Key Partners

Key partners are organized by which ones are required regardless of revenue model, and which ones are contingent on the path chosen.

### Required in any version of this venture

**Clinical advisor — registered dietitian or certified diabetes educator**
The assumption scorecard identified incorrect glycemic guidance as a liability risk. The pattern analysis confirmed that the target customer has partial and inconsistent blood sugar knowledge — they will not reliably catch an error in the system's recommendations. This means the accuracy of the glycemic logic cannot rest on user correction. A credentialed clinical partner who reviews and validates the blood sugar rules embedded in the AI's output is not optional. Without one, the product is making medical-adjacent recommendations without accountability, which is both an ethical and legal exposure.

This partner also serves the AI trust problem from the Customer Relationships block. Grace found a diabetes app irrelevant to her demographic. Nelsy trusts her mother over apps. A named clinical advisor — "reviewed by a certified diabetes educator" — does not fully solve the trust problem, but it provides a credibility signal the product cannot generate from the AI output alone.

What this partner is not: a full-time medical staff. At early stage, this is likely a consulting relationship with a single RD or CDE who reviews the glycemic logic library before launch and periodically as the product updates.

**AI inference provider**
The core product generation runs on a large language model. This is the technical prerequisite for the "why now" argument: prior to AI capable of reasoning across budget, perishability, schedule, and blood sugar constraints simultaneously, this product could not exist. The specific provider is a vendor dependency — a change in pricing, availability, or capability affects the product directly. This should be treated as a critical infrastructure dependency, not a commodity service.

**Grocery price data source**
The budget constraint is the hardest limit confirmed in the interviews. Grace's $60 ceiling is a fact, not a preference. A plan that tells her to buy ingredients at prices she cannot afford is a plan she will not follow. Real, local, current grocery prices are a data requirement for the product to function as described. This can be sourced three ways: a direct grocery chain partnership (Remix 3), a third-party grocery data aggregator such as Flipp or a retail API, or user-supplied price entry. The third option adds friction at setup. The second is a data vendor relationship. The first solves the data problem and the payer problem simultaneously — which is the structural reason Remix 3 remains the most attractive long-term path.

---

### Contingent on revenue model

**Regional grocery chain (Remix 3 path)**
If the grocery partnership model is pursued, the chain becomes the venture's most important single partner: payer, distribution channel, and data source combined. The venture's success in this model is contingent on identifying a chain with a pre-diabetic or health-conscious customer segment, a community liaison or health initiative already in place, and an internal champion willing to move a pilot through procurement. None of that is fast. Identifying the right chain and the right internal contact is the first activity in this path, not the last.

**Community health clinic, FQHC, or employer (Remix 2 path)**
If the institutional model is pursued, the clinic or employer is the paying customer and the referral channel simultaneously. The financial case is strong — preventing diabetes progression saves the institution significant downstream treatment cost. The sales challenge is access: these relationships are typically built through existing networks, not cold outreach. No interview evidence validates this partner relationship exists or is accessible from the venture's current position.

**None required for direct-to-consumer (Remix 1 path)**
The direct-to-consumer path requires the clinical advisor, AI provider, and grocery data source listed above, but no third-party institutional partner. It trades partner dependency for customer acquisition cost and the pricing constraints documented in Block 5.

## Block 8: Key Activities

Key activities split into two categories: those required to build and maintain the core product, and those required to reach and sustain the customer. The second category depends on revenue model.

### Core product activities — required in every version

**Generating personalized weekly meal plans**
The central technical activity: taking a family's budget, current grocery prices, fridge inventory, weekly schedule, and blood sugar constraints and producing a ready-to-execute weekly plan with a matched grocery list. This is what the product is. The quality of this output is the entire value proposition. Every interview confirmed the failure happens at the execution step — inspiration without a ready-to-use output does not change behavior. The output must be complete, specific, and usable without additional research or conversion work on the customer's part.

**Building and maintaining the glycemic logic library**
The rules that make meal plans blood-sugar-safe are not generated fresh by an AI each time — they need to be curated, validated against clinical standards, and maintained as nutritional guidance and AI capabilities evolve. This is an ongoing activity, not a one-time build. It is also the venture's primary defensible asset. A generic meal planner cannot claim this; a pre-diabetic food system built on validated glycemic logic can. Letting this library go stale or unvalidated is the fastest path to the liability exposure identified in the Cost Structure block.

**Onboarding and plan setup**
Collecting the inputs needed for personalization — budget, family size, food preferences, schedule, what's already in the fridge — must happen before the first plan is generated. The interview evidence is unambiguous that friction at this step will produce abandonment: Veronica found a free app felt like homework, every structured tool in the interview set was tried and abandoned. Onboarding must be designed to front-load the necessary input once and reduce re-engagement requirements to a minimum. This is a product design activity with direct retention implications.

**Validating recommendations for accuracy and safety**
Before any output reaches a user managing a real medical condition, the system's recommendations need to be checked for clinical accuracy. This is both a launch activity — the glycemic logic library must be validated before first use — and an ongoing QA activity as the system is updated. This is where the clinical partner relationship is operationalized.

---

### Go-to-market activities — depend on revenue model

**Short-form content creation (direct-to-consumer path)**
Three of five interviewees use TikTok or YouTube for food help. Content that intercepts the target customer at the moment she hits the execution gap — she's watching a recipe she can't make because she's missing two ingredients — and demonstrates that the system closes that gap meets her where she already is. This is the awareness channel most directly supported by the interview data. It requires consistent production, not a one-time campaign.

**B2B partnership development (institutional or grocery path)**
Identifying the right institutional or grocery chain partner, finding the internal champion, building the pilot proposal, and moving through procurement. This is a high-effort, long-timeline activity with a 6–12 month horizon before a first contract. It requires a fundamentally different skill set than content creation and cannot run on the same timeline as product validation.

**Willingness-to-pay testing (immediate priority, all paths)**
No interviewee was asked what they would pay. The assumption scorecard flagged this as a gap. Before the revenue model can be chosen with any confidence, the target customer needs to be asked directly. This is an interview activity, not a product activity, and it should happen before the next major product decision is made.

## Block 9: Key Resources

### Intellectual property

**Validated glycemic logic library**
The curated, clinically validated set of blood sugar rules that power the meal plan recommendations. This is the asset that distinguishes this product from a generic meal planner with a diabetes label. It is expensive and time-consuming to build correctly — it requires clinical expertise and ongoing maintenance — which means it is also the asset hardest for a better-funded competitor to replicate quickly. Everything else in the product can be rebuilt faster by a larger team. This cannot.

---

### Technical infrastructure

**AI inference capability**
Access to a model capable of reasoning across budget, perishability, schedule, and blood sugar constraints simultaneously. This is the technical prerequisite that makes the product possible now when it was not possible before. As noted in the venture direction decision, the "why now" argument depends entirely on this capability existing. It is a resource the venture accesses through a vendor relationship, not one it owns — which means the dependency risk should be managed.

**Grocery price and inventory data**
Real, local, current pricing data. Without it, the budget constraint cannot be honored in the meal plan output and the system cannot be built around what is actually on sale this week. This resource is either purchased from a data vendor, supplied by a grocery chain partner, or collected through user input at the cost of additional friction.

---

### Human capital

**Clinical advisor**
A registered dietitian or certified diabetes educator who validates the glycemic logic, reviews the recommendation output before launch, and provides ongoing clinical oversight. Named in Key Partners as a required relationship. Listed here as a resource because the venture's liability exposure, product credibility, and AI trust problem all depend on this person existing and being engaged.

**The founder's personal experience**
The systems analysis documented this directly: the founder pivoted to this problem because she experiences it personally and has direct, unencumbered access to validate it. This is a genuine resource. It provides domain knowledge that cannot be purchased at early stage, direct access to the target customer community, and lived understanding of the failure modes the product is designed to solve. It also provides a testing advantage — the founder can evaluate whether a generated meal plan would actually work in a real pre-diabetic household without recruiting a test user for every iteration.

This resource has limits. Personal experience validates the problem. It does not validate the clinical accuracy of the solution. The founder's blood sugar knowledge is subject to the same gaps identified in the interview data — it should not substitute for the clinical advisor in validating glycemic recommendations.
