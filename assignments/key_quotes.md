# Key Quotes from Customer Discovery
BUS 395 | Fanny Polanco | Summer 2026
Source: Polanco_Week3_Interview_Transcripts_Final.docx — five interviews

---

## 1. Tere (Interview 1)
> "I have the intention, but not a good system."

The clearest confirmation of the venture's core premise in the entire data set. The customer is not unmotivated — she is unsupported. This quote names the exact gap the product is built to fill, in her words, without prompting.

---

## 2. Tere (Interview 1)
> "I know the rice isn't great for my sugar, but I was too tired to think of something else."

Challenges the assumption that behavior change follows from knowledge. Tere has the relevant information and ignores it — not from indifference but from exhaustion and the competing constraint that rice is what her children will eat. This reveals that the unit of intervention is the household, not just the individual, and that the product needs to remove the decision point entirely rather than inform it.

---

## 3. Tere (Interview 1)
> "I feel guilty throwing them away, but it happens every week."

Produce waste is emotional, not just financial. The guilt signal matters for messaging: "stop wasting money" and "stop feeling guilty every week" are different value propositions aimed at different motivators. For customers who already know what they should do and keep failing, guilt may be the more resonant hook.

---

## 4. Mary (Interview 2)
> "I had oatmeal for breakfast because it's 'healthy,' even though I'm not sure about the sugar."

**Challenges the idea.** The original problem statement claims the problem is not nutritional knowledge. Mary's answer contradicts that directly — she is operating on incomplete and possibly incorrect information about which foods affect blood sugar. The knowledge gap and the system gap appear to coexist, which complicates a solution that assumes users can evaluate recommendations without foundational context.

---

## 5. Nelsy (Interview 4)
> "I call mom and she gives me these recipes. They are healthy, but I don't end up cooking them."

**The most important challenge in the data set.** Nelsy's mother is a trusted, personalized, human source of healthy guidance — and it still does not convert to action. If trusted human advice fails to bridge to execution, the venture cannot assume that trusted AI guidance will either. The product's job is not to earn trust; it is to deliver an output so complete and ready to execute that the step from receiving it to acting on it requires minimal additional work.

---

## 6. Grace (Interview 3)
> "I tried a diabetes app once, but it felt like it was made for older people. Not helpful."

**Challenges the idea.** An existing product in this space lost Grace not on features or accuracy but on cultural and demographic fit. Design, tone, and visual identity are adoption factors — not secondary polish. Any product that reads as clinical or age-mismatched will face the same rejection, regardless of how technically sound the meal recommendations are.

---

## 7. Veronica (Interview 5)
> "I tried a free meal prep app, but it felt like homework. Too many notifications."

**Challenges the idea.** Veronica abandoned a solution at zero cost. Price is not the only adoption barrier — possibly not even the primary one. A product that requires ongoing engagement, repeated input, or active check-ins imposes a friction cost that overrides free. The design principle this demands: deliver a complete, usable output without asking the customer to do more than she agreed to do at sign-up.

---

## 8. Veronica (Interview 5)
> "I don't like vegetables. I know I need to eat them though."

**Challenges the idea.** Every other interviewee aspires to eat vegetables even when they fail to. Veronica is motivated toward a health outcome but not toward the foods that produce it. A blood-sugar-safe meal plan built around vegetables is still a plan she will not follow. This does not invalidate the venture — it defines the boundary of the addressable customer. Some pre-diabetic adults cannot be reached by a food planning system because the barrier is preference, not logistics.
