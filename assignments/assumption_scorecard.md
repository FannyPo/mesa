## Assumption 1: The market is saturated with pre-diabetic tools

**Evidence for it**
- PlateJoy built a CDC-recognized Diabetes Prevention Program specifically for pre-diabetic users and raised $12.3M — proof the category attracted real investment.
- Klinio and eMeals exist and carry diabetes-friendly labels.
- Tools were findable through competitive research, suggesting the space is not invisible.

**Evidence against it**
- Gap analysis finding: "there is genuinely no product on the market built specifically for pre-diabetics as a primary consumer customer. Every tool either labels a general wellness product with a diabetes-friendly tag or, like PlateJoy and Klinio, targets a clinical or insurance channel."
- Grace tried a diabetes app and found it "felt like it was made for older people. Not helpful." — the app existed but was not built for her.
- Mary tried a low carb cookbook, not a diabetes-specific tool — suggesting she could not find or did not reach for a dedicated pre-diabetic product.
- Across all five interviews, not one person named a tool they are currently using for blood sugar management. Zero active users of a pre-diabetic product in the sample.

**Verdict: Disproven**

The tools that exist target clinical channels or insurance reimbursement. At the direct-to-consumer level, where this venture would operate, the space is largely empty. This was an assumption built on surface-level pattern matching — diabetes is a known category, therefore tools exist — without checking whether those tools reach the actual consumer.

**What to change**
Stop treating competition as the primary risk. The risk is not that the market is crowded; it is that the market has been consistently underserved because serving it well is genuinely hard. The absence of competition is not an open lane — it is a signal worth investigating.

## Assumption 2: Pricing is a secondary differentiator

**Evidence for it**
- None from the interviews. No interviewee said price was not a concern.

**Evidence against it**
- Nelsy: "I tried a meal kit once, but it was too expensive. I canceled after two weeks." — tried a structured solution, abandoned it specifically because of cost.
- Grace: "I went with a strict budget — $60." and "I wanted to buy healthier stuff, but it didn't fit the budget." — $60 is a ceiling, not a preference. Any system that increases upfront grocery cost fails before launch.
- Mary: "I spent more than I wanted to" — overspending is a stated pain point, not background noise.
- Competitive analysis finding: "subscription pricing across every direct competitor is structurally incompatible with the target customer. At $5–15 per month before adding real grocery or delivery costs, these products are not inconvenient for this customer; they are a complete barrier to entry."
- PlateJoy's B2B pivot was driven in part by the impossibility of charging the pre-diabetic consumer directly.

**Verdict: Disproven**

Price is not a differentiator — it is a gate. Three of five interviewees surfaced cost as a direct constraint on their food and tool decisions. The competitive research confirmed the same. This assumption was not just wrong; it was the structural reason every prior product in this category failed to reach the target customer.

**What to change**
Pricing must be a primary design constraint, not a positioning decision made after the product is built. The business model needs to answer how this reaches a customer who cannot reliably afford $10/month before the core product is validated. Remix 2 (clinic/employer funding) and Remix 3 (grocery chain subsidy) exist precisely because of this problem — do not treat them as optional future layers.

## Assumption 3: My target customer understands basic blood sugar logic

**Evidence for it**
- Tere: "I know the rice isn't great for my sugar." One food identified correctly.
- Grace: "I know ramen is bad for sugar." One food identified correctly.
- Mary tried a low carb cookbook — she knows low-carb is directionally relevant to her condition.

**Evidence against it**
- Mary: "I had oatmeal for breakfast because it's 'healthy,' even though I'm not sure about the sugar." — direct admission of uncertainty about a food that appears on most pre-diabetic guidance lists.
- Mary ate Mac and Cheese for dinner with no apparent blood sugar flag — same carbohydrate problem as the rice she avoided, not recognized.
- Grace knows ramen is bad but bought bread and rice on the same trip with no concern — the logic does not transfer across foods.
- Grace: "I try to go walking" as a response to eating ramen — suggests she believes exercise offsets dietary risk, which is not reliable blood sugar management.
- Veronica bought things that "seemed healthy" with no blood sugar framework underneath — no food in her interview is evaluated through a blood sugar lens.
- Veronica had yogurt for breakfast with no consideration of sugar content.
- Nelsy's entire dietary framework is sodium. Zero blood sugar awareness.
- None of the five mentioned glycemic index, carb portions, fiber, meal timing, or the difference between simple and complex carbohydrates.

**Verdict: Partially confirmed — and the partial is doing a lot of work**

Two of five interviewees can name one bad food each. Neither can apply that logic consistently across similar foods. One interviewee has no blood sugar framework at all. One is not pre-diabetic. What exists is a short list of memorized bad foods without the underlying logic that would allow someone to make a new decision at the store or recognize when a meal plan recommendation is right or wrong.

**What to change**
The problem statement claim — "the problem is not a lack of nutritional knowledge" — is not supported by these five interviews. Do not repeat it without qualification. At minimum, the product's recommendations need to be legible to someone with patchy knowledge: explain the reasoning briefly so customers can partially verify what they are being asked to do. If they cannot evaluate the output at all, they will either override it when it conflicts with their intuitions or follow it blindly — both create risk.

## Assumption 4: The core problem is a system breakdown, not a knowledge gap

**Evidence for it**
- Tere: "I have the intention, but not a good system." — the clearest statement of the problem across all five interviews.
- All five confirmed weekly produce waste, grocery shopping without a list, and abandonment of at least one tool or resource — the structural failure pattern held across every interview.
- Tere knows rice is bad and eats it every night anyway. Grace knows ramen is bad and eats it for lunch. In both cases, knowledge is present and the system still fails — confirming the system breakdown exists independently of the knowledge gap.
- Mary overspends even when she is not buying healthier food — the absence of a plan drives inefficiency regardless of food choices, which is a system failure, not a knowledge failure.
- The inspiration-to-execution gap confirmed across all five: YouTube, TikTok, Pinterest, and family recipes all fail to convert to meals. The information exists. The bridge to action does not.

**Evidence against it**
- Mary is unsure whether oatmeal is safe. A system that tells her to eat oatmeal three times a week will not resolve her confusion — it will require her to either trust the system completely or do external research to verify it.
- Veronica has no blood sugar framework. She cannot use a blood-sugar-safe system she cannot evaluate. For her, the knowledge gap comes before the system gap.
- Grace believes walking compensates for ramen. A system cannot correct that belief by generating a better meal plan. The belief will persist and will affect how she interprets and uses the output.
- Nelsy trusts her mother's healthy recipes and still does not cook them — suggesting that even when good information reaches a trusted source and converts into a concrete recommendation, it does not produce action. The implementation gap may be more than a system problem.

**Verdict: Partially confirmed**

The system breakdown is real. It was confirmed across all five interviews without exception and is the most consistent finding in the data. But "not a knowledge gap" is too strong. A knowledge gap coexists in at least three of five cases, and in one case (Veronica) it may be the more pressing barrier. The more accurate framing from this data: system gap is primary, knowledge gap is secondary and real.

**What to change**
Retire the absolute framing. "The problem is not a lack of nutritional knowledge" overstates what the data supports and will become a liability when investors or advisors push back. Replace it with something the evidence actually supports: the system gap is the primary, addressable barrier for the target customer. The knowledge gap is real but narrow enough — in Profile 1 customers — that a well-designed system can work around it without becoming a nutrition education platform.

## Assumption 5: Pre-diabetic parents would trust an AI system for food planning

**Evidence for it**
- Tere: wants guidance but "not something time-consuming" — openness to a low-friction solution.
- Mary: abandoned a cookbook because recipes were "too complicated" — desire for something simpler exists.
- Grace: paralyzed by Google overload, wants a "trusted, filtered, personal recommendation" — language that describes what an AI system could provide.
- The universal failure of existing tools creates a gap the target customer is aware of and frustrated by — motivation to try something new is present.

**Evidence against it**
- Grace found a diabetes app that "felt like it was made for older people. Not helpful." — distrust of existing digital tools is the baseline, not trust.
- Nelsy trusts her mother over apps. Good information from a trusted, familiar human source still did not produce action — and that source is a person, not an algorithm.
- Veronica found a free app felt "like homework. Too many notifications." — apps create friction and obligation rather than trust, even at zero cost.
- Mary abandoned a cookbook for being too complicated. None of the tools tried were AI-powered, but the pattern of trying and abandoning structured tools is consistent enough to be a warning.
- The competitive analysis flagged this as an open question that interviews needed to confirm: "Would pre-diabetic parents trust AI to make food decisions for their family's health?" — no interview asked about AI directly.

**Verdict: Inconclusive**

This assumption was never tested. No interviewee was asked about AI. What the interviews do show is that trust in digital tools generally is low — every tool tried was abandoned, and the one interviewee who names a trusted source names her mother. The openness to a better solution is real. Whether "AI" specifically triggers trust or suspicion in this customer for a medically sensitive condition is unknown.

**What to change**
This must be tested in the next round of interviews before any AI-first product decision is locked in. A direct question — "if an app used AI to build your weekly meal plan around your blood sugar, budget, and schedule, what would have to be true for you to trust it?" — will surface what conditions trust requires. Building an AI-first product without that answer is the highest unresolved risk in the venture.
