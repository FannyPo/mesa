### The Three Revenue Models and Their Tradeoffs

#### Option 1: Direct-to-Consumer Subscription

The pre-diabetic adult pays a monthly fee directly.

**Revenue logic:** Clean model, shortest path to revenue, full control over the product experience and customer relationship. No intermediary, no long sales cycle.

**Problem:** The customer segment confirmed across three interviews is structurally price-sensitive. Subscription pricing at any level that sustains a software business requires a customer willing and able to pay monthly. Grace's $60 grocery ceiling and Nelsy's abandoned meal kit suggest this customer prioritizes her grocery budget over a planning tool. If the product cannot demonstrate immediate, tangible savings — less wasted food, less overspending — the subscription fee competes with grocery dollars.

**Floor price that makes the model viable:** Unknown. No interviewee was asked. This must be tested before this option can be evaluated seriously.

---

#### Option 2: Institutional B2B — Clinic or Employer Pays

A community health clinic, FQHC, or employer benefits program funds free access for pre-diabetic patients or employees. The pre-diabetic family manager uses the product free of charge.

**Revenue logic:** The financial case for an institutional payer is strong. A single patient progressing from pre-diabetes to Type 2 diabetes costs an estimated $9,600–$16,000 per year in treatment. A prevention tool at $30–50 per member per month has an asymmetric ROI for any payer managing downstream diabetes costs. PlateJoy validated that institutional payers will engage with this category — it raised $12.3M partly on this logic.

PlateJoy's failure in this model was specific: it routed through insurance reimbursement, which added HIPAA constraints on communication and tied revenue to behavior outcomes it could not control. A direct contract with a clinic or employer, without insurance intermediation, avoids both failure modes.

**Problem:** Sales cycles are 6–12 months minimum for institutional deals. This model has zero interview validation — no interviewee mentioned a clinic or employer as a source of food guidance or health support. The financial logic is sound. The pathway to a first contract is unproven.

---

#### Option 3: Grocery Chain Partnership

A regional grocery chain funds free or subsidized access for shoppers. The system is built around the chain's weekly sales circular and local inventory. The chain captures a loyal, intentional shopper making predictable weekly purchases. The user pays nothing and downloads nothing new.

**Revenue logic:** The grocery chain's incentive is direct — basket size lift and shopper loyalty. A pre-diabetic shopper whose weekly plan is built around the store's sales produces a predictable, higher-value weekly basket than a shopper without a plan. The licensing or partnership fee comes from the chain, not the consumer.

This model solves the payer problem and the channel problem simultaneously. It is the structurally cleanest option for reaching this customer.

**Problem:** Grocery chains move slowly. Finding and closing a pilot partnership requires an internal champion, procurement cycles, and a proof of concept. None of that is fast. The competitive analysis flagged this risk explicitly: a pilot deal could exceed the venture timeline.

### Who Captures the Most Value and Who Is Most Likely to Pay

The entity with the greatest financial upside from preventing diabetes progression is an **institutional payer** — an employer or health plan managing a population with high pre-diabetes prevalence. The ROI math is strongly in their favor. They are also the slowest to move and the hardest to access without an existing relationship.

The entity most likely to pay quickly is the **direct consumer** — but the evidence says she cannot reliably pay enough to make unit economics work at standard software margins.

The **grocery chain** sits between: meaningful financial incentive, faster to engage than a health plan, and the partnership model sidesteps the consumer price sensitivity problem. It is the most structurally attractive option and the least evidentially validated.

### Recommended Sequencing

*Stated as a hypothesis, not a validated plan.*

Use direct-to-consumer (very low price or free with a clear upgrade path) to validate that the product works and that users follow the plan. Use that evidence — retention rate, plan follow-through, measurable waste reduction — to sell the institutional or grocery channel. The consumer validates the product. The institution or chain provides the sustainable revenue.

This sequencing is not supported by interview evidence. It is a logical inference from the evidence that does exist. It should be tested.
