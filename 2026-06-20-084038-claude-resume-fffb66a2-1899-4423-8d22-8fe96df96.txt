  What the customer already pays for alternatives:

  ---
  Option 2: One-Time Purchase

  Evidence for it:
  Lower psychological barrier than a recurring charge. Might clear the "another
  monthly bill" objection.

  Evidence against it:
  The value of this product is weekly regeneration — new inputs every week, new
  plan. A one-time purchase doesn't match a product whose core value is ongoing.
  There's no behavioral evidence any interviewee would pay once for a recurring
  problem.

  Verdict: The product structure doesn't support this model.

  ---
  Option 3: Freemium

  Evidence for it:
  Veronica quit a free app for friction, not cost. That's actually a useful
  signal — it means the freemium tier would have to deliver genuine, complete
  value on first use, not a teaser. If the free plan is genuinely useful,
  adoption barrier drops.

  Evidence against it:
  Freemium only generates revenue if free users convert to paid. This customer
  segment converts to paid at near-zero rates based on income constraints. You'd
  be building a free product for an audience that can't upgrade. And Veronica
  proves that "free but friction-heavy" still fails — so the free tier has to be
  frictionless, which makes it harder to justify the paid upgrade.

  What they pay for alternatives:
  Nothing. The free tier would compete with free YouTube, which is already
  losing.

  Option 4: Transaction Fee (per plan generated)

  Pay-when-you-use aligns with inconsistent engagement — no one is a consistent
