# Cross-Interview Pattern Analysis
BUS 395 | Fanny Polanco | Summer 2026
Source: Polanco_Week3_Interview_Transcripts_Final.docx — all five interviews

---

## Confirmed Patterns
*Three or more interviewees said independently, without being prompted by the same question framing*

---

**Pattern 1: Weekly produce waste is universal and accepted as inevitable**
Every single interviewee confirmed this. Not as a problem they are trying to solve — as a recurring fact of their life.

- Tere: "I buy vegetables with good intentions, but by the time I get around to cooking, they're already soft or moldy. I feel guilty throwing them away, but it happens every week."
- Mary: "Yes. Especially spinach and berries. They go bad so fast. I buy them because I know I should eat healthier, but I never plan meals around them."
- Grace: "I buy veggies thinking I'll cook, but then I'm too tired after work. They sit in the fridge until they're gross."
- Nelsy: "Yes. Especially things like zucchini or herbs. I buy them for one recipe and then don't use the rest."
- Veronica: "Yes. I buy fruit and veggies, but I forget they're there. I don't know how to use them before they spoil."

Five for five. The cause varies — exhaustion (Tere, Grace), forgetting (Veronica), single-recipe purchasing (Nelsy), missing a meal plan (Mary) — but the outcome is identical. This is the most consistently confirmed finding in the data set and the strongest evidence that a waste-prevention mechanism belongs at the center of the value proposition.

---

**Pattern 2: Grocery shopping happens without a list or a meal plan**
Every interviewee described the same shopping behavior: going to the store with either no plan or a vague intention that does not translate into specific meals.

- Tere: "I went after work, already exhausted. I didn't have a list. I just walked around grabbing things I usually buy."
- Mary: "I tried to buy healthier things, but I didn't know what meals to make. I ended up buying the same stuff I always buy."
- Grace: "I went with a strict budget — $60. I bought basics: eggs, bread, chicken, rice."
- Nelsy: "I walked around and bought the main things we always eat."
- Veronica: "I walked in with an idea of what I should buy, and I bought things that 'seemed healthy,' but I didn't know what meals to make."

The pattern inside the pattern: every interviewee considered buying healthier food and did not, because they could not connect the healthier item to a specific meal they would actually make. The decision to put something back is not about cost alone — it is about not knowing what to do with it. This directly supports the ingredient-first planning mechanism as a point-of-purchase intervention, not just a Sunday planning tool.

---

**Pattern 3: Inspiration does not convert to action — across every platform**
Every interviewee uses at least one content source for food ideas. Not one of them converts that content into meals consistently.

- Tere: YouTube and TikTok — "they use ingredients I don't have."
- Mary: TikTok — "those recipes look good and then I never make them. I think I have all the ingredients and then I'm always missing a couple of them."
- Grace: Google — "it gives too many answers and I don't know which one to look at. I feel like I spend a lot of time."
- Nelsy: her mother — "I call mom and she gives me these recipes. They are healthy, but I don't end up cooking them."
- Veronica: Pinterest — "But I never actually cook the recipes."

The failure mode varies: ingredient mismatch (Tere, Mary), information overload (Grace), implementation gap (Nelsy), passive saving without execution (Veronica). But the outcome is the same across all five sources — TikTok, YouTube, Pinterest, Google, and a trusted human being. This is the strongest argument for the venture's core premise: the problem is not access to food ideas. It is the bridge from idea to executed meal. That bridge does not exist anywhere in this customer's current ecosystem.

---

**Pattern 4: Every structured resource was tried and abandoned**
All five interviewees tried at least one tool or structured resource and stopped using it.

- Tere: YouTube and TikTok content — ingredient mismatch breaks execution.
- Mary: a low-carb cookbook — "the recipes were too complicated. I don't have time for all that prep."
- Grace: a diabetes app — "it felt like it was made for older people. Not helpful."
- Nelsy: a meal kit — "it was too expensive. I canceled after two weeks."
- Veronica: a free meal prep app — "it felt like homework. Too many notifications."

Five different tools. Five different failure reasons: complexity, demographic irrelevance, price, and friction. The variety of failure modes matters. If every tool failed for the same reason, the fix would be obvious. The fact that they failed for different reasons suggests the problem is not fixable by improving any one dimension of an existing tool. It requires a different product category.

What makes this pattern a challenge to the venture: this customer has tried and abandoned every category of solution that exists. A new tool — however well-designed — enters a market where the customer's default response to new tools is "I've been here before." The trust problem in the AI assumption scorecard is downstream of this pattern.

---

**Pattern 5: Speed and ease override health when there is no plan**
When the plan breaks down — which it does regularly — every interviewee defaults to whatever is fastest, not whatever is healthiest.

- Tere: "I was too tired to think of something else" → rice and beans every night.
- Mary: "Lunch was fast food because I was running errands."
- Grace: "I ate whatever was cheap. Eggs for breakfast, ramen for lunch, and a frozen meal for dinner."
- Nelsy: "I decide based on what's fast."
- Veronica: "I didn't plan anything. I just went with whatever was easiest." → skipped lunch, ordered takeout.

This is a systems finding, not a motivation finding. None of these people said they do not care about their health. The default to fast food is not a preference — it is what happens when the planning system is absent. This confirms the venture's core premise: the problem is structural, not motivational. The fix is a system that removes the decision point, not a nudge that reminds them to make a better one.

---

**Pattern 6: Overspending despite not buying healthier food**
At least three interviewees spent more than they intended at the grocery store without filling their cart with healthy food.

- Mary: "I tried to buy healthier things, but I didn't know what meals to make. I ended up buying the same stuff I always buy. I spent more than I wanted to."
- Veronica: "I bought things that 'seemed healthy,' but I didn't know what meals to make. I spent too much."
- Tere: (implied) looked at healthier options, put them back as too expensive — suggesting the budget limit was already strained by the regular items.

The mechanism is consistent: without a plan, the cart fills with familiar defaults, overspending happens anyway, and the healthier items that were considered do not make it in. The customer loses money without gaining the health benefit she was reaching for. This is a value proposition argument that has not been fully developed in the current documents — the system saves money not by being cheap but by making the existing grocery budget do what it was intended to do.

## Contradictions
*Where interviewees disagreed with each other, or where what they said did not match what they did*

---

**Contradiction 1: They know a specific food is bad for them and eat it every day**

This is the sharpest contradiction in the data set and the one most directly in tension with the venture's premise.

- Tere: "I know the rice isn't great for my sugar, but I was too tired to think of something else." She eats rice every night.
- Grace: "I know ramen is bad for sugar, but it's cheap and fast." She eats ramen for lunch.

Both interviewees have the relevant knowledge. Neither changes the behavior. The system fails not because they do not know — it fails because knowing is not sufficient to act differently when exhausted, budget-constrained, or feeding children who want what they want.

This is a challenge to the venture because it raises the question: if knowledge does not produce behavior change for Tere and Grace today, what makes a planning system different? The answer has to be that the system removes the decision entirely rather than informing it — she does not have to choose between rice and something better if the plan already has a better option prepped and accessible. But that mechanism depends on the customer following the plan in advance, which is the behavior the venture is trying to produce. The circularity is real.

---

**Contradiction 2: The stated problem is "I don't know what to eat" but the revealed problem is "I know and can't act on it"**

Every interviewee, when asked what they eat, demonstrates they have a working food framework. Tere knows rice is not ideal but defaults to it. Grace knows ramen is bad but eats it. Mary knows she should eat healthier but buys the same cart. Veronica knows she needs vegetables but does not like them.

The stated need — "I don't know what to eat" — is a surface complaint. The revealed need is that they know what they should eat and cannot make it happen consistently under real conditions. These are not the same problem. The first is an information problem. The second is an execution problem.

This mostly supports the venture — the system gap is real. But it also means "knowing what to eat" is not the gap the product needs to close, and any marketing that promises to teach them what to eat will land wrong with this customer. She already knows. She needs a system, not a lesson.

---

**Contradiction 3: Grace's blood sugar knowledge does not transfer across similar foods**

Grace knows ramen is bad for her blood sugar. On the same grocery trip, she bought bread and rice with no apparent flag. Bread and rice create the same glycemic problem as ramen. The knowledge does not generalize — it is attached to a specific food (ramen), not to an underlying principle (refined carbohydrates spike blood sugar).

This directly contradicts the idea that Profile 1 customers have enough foundational knowledge to work with. Grace cannot apply her one piece of correct knowledge to a new food. A system that surfaces brief rationale for its recommendations — as recommended in the Value Propositions block — cannot assume the customer will recognize "bread is similar to ramen" without that being stated explicitly. The partial knowledge is patchier than the profile description suggests.

---

**Contradiction 4: Nelsy receives trusted, personalized, healthy guidance from her mother and does not act on it**

This is the contradiction that most directly challenges the venture's customer relationship model. Nelsy's mother gives her healthy recipes. The source is trusted. The recipes are good. The advice is personalized. She does not cook them.

If trusted, personalized guidance from a human being does not bridge to execution, the venture cannot assume that trusted, personalized guidance from an AI system will either. The failure is not in the source or the quality of the advice — it is in the gap between receiving a recommendation and completing the work of turning it into a meal. The product needs to deliver a ready-to-execute output, not guidance. The distinction matters in product design. Guidance requires the customer to do something. A complete output does not.

---

**Contradiction 5: Veronica is pre-diabetic and does not like vegetables**

Every other interviewee aspires to eat vegetables even when they fail to. Veronica stated directly: "I don't like vegetables. I know I need to eat them though."

This contradicts the implicit assumption that the target customer is motivated toward the foods the product would recommend. Veronica is motivated toward a health outcome but not toward the foods that produce it. A blood-sugar-safe meal plan built around vegetables and whole foods is still a plan she will not follow if the vegetables are the obstacle. Palatability is a separate barrier from planning, and the product cannot solve it.

This does not disqualify the venture — it disqualifies Veronica as the primary target customer, which is consistent with the narrowing in the Customer Segments block. But it is worth stating plainly: some pre-diabetic adults will not be reached by a food planning system regardless of its quality, because the barrier is preference, not logistics.

## Surprises
*Things the data revealed that were not anticipated in the original problem framing*

---

**Surprise 1: Produce waste carries guilt, not just financial frustration**

The original problem framing positioned produce waste as a financial and logistical failure. The interviews revealed it is also an emotional one.

- Tere: "I feel guilty throwing them away, but it happens every week."

She is not primarily frustrated by the cost. She feels guilty — which implies she is measuring herself against a standard she believes in and failing to meet it regularly. Grace and others described the waste matter-of-factly, but Tere's guilt signal stands out. This matters for messaging: "stop wasting money" and "stop feeling guilty every week" are different value propositions aimed at different emotional drivers. The guilt framing may be more motivating for the customer who already knows what she should do and keeps failing to do it.

---

**Surprise 2: Family food preferences are a hard constraint that the original problem statement missed entirely**

Tere: "I made rice, beans, and chicken for dinner because it's what the kids will eat. I know the rice isn't great for my sugar, but I was too tired to think of something else."

The original problem framing described the pre-diabetic adult as the unit of analysis. Tere's answer shows the unit is the household. She is not making food decisions for herself — she is making food decisions for a family, and the family's preferences override her individual health logic every night she is too tired to fight it.

A blood-sugar-safe meal plan that the children will not eat is a plan that does not get followed. This was not anticipated in the original framing and has not been fully addressed in the product concept. The system needs to solve for household compatibility, not just individual blood sugar safety. That is a harder product.

---

**Surprise 3: A free product was abandoned for reasons that had nothing to do with price**

Veronica: "I tried a free meal prep app, but it felt like homework. Too many notifications."

The assumption scorecard confirmed that price is a structural barrier for this customer. Veronica's case shows that removing price entirely does not solve the adoption problem. The friction from notifications and ongoing engagement demands drove abandonment at zero cost.

This is a direct challenge to any strategy that treats "make it cheaper" as the primary lever for adoption. The product design problem — low friction, pull-based, delivers a complete output without requiring ongoing engagement — is as important as the pricing problem, and it cannot be deferred to a later product iteration.

---

**Surprise 4: No interviewee mentioned a doctor or healthcare provider as a food resource**

All five interviewees are pre-diabetic or high risk. Not one of them mentioned their doctor, a nurse, a dietitian referral, or any healthcare provider in response to any question about where they go for food help.

The sources named across all five interviews: TikTok, YouTube, Pinterest, Google, a cookbook, a diabetes app, a meal kit, and a mother. Healthcare providers are absent from the entire data set. This means either doctors are not giving food guidance to pre-diabetic patients at the point of diagnosis, or the guidance is given and does not stick, or it does not reach the patient as something actionable enough to name when asked.

This is a channel opportunity — a healthcare provider referral at the point of pre-diabetes diagnosis is a high-credibility, high-urgency moment — but it has zero direct support from the interviews because none of the five interviewees named it. Any channel strategy that relies on HCP referral is built on an inference, not on evidence from this data set.

---

**Surprise 5: Grace is already trying to solve the problem herself — and her solution created a new problem**

Grace: "I buy veggies thinking I'll cook, but then I'm too tired after work. They sit in the fridge until they're gross, or I cook them all at once to eat over a couple of days and they get soggy."

Grace identified the waste problem, developed a workaround (batch cooking), and discovered that the workaround produced a palatability failure by day three. She is not passive. She is actively problem-solving and running into the limits of what an individual can solve without a real system.

This is surprising because the original problem framing described the customer as someone who has given up or defaulted. Grace has not given up. She tried a solution and hit a wall the solution could not clear. The product needs to account for her specific failure mode — texture degradation across multiple days — not just the planning failure that precedes it. Ingredient-first planning solves the buying problem. It does not automatically solve what happens when broccoli is soggy on Thursday.

---

**Surprise 6: The trust problem may not be about the source at all**

The assumption scorecard flagged AI trust as inconclusive. What the interviews revealed — particularly Nelsy's case — suggests the trust problem may be a red herring.

Nelsy trusts her mother completely. Her mother gives her good recipes. She does not cook them. The trusted human source fails for the same reason every other source fails: the gap is not between the customer and the source. It is between receiving a recommendation and completing the work of execution.

If that is true, asking "will she trust an AI?" is the wrong question. The right question is: "will she execute what the system gives her?" And the interviews suggest execution depends on the output being ready to use, not on the customer trusting the source that generated it. This does not eliminate the AI trust question — medical recommendations require a different threshold than recipe suggestions — but it reframes it. Trust may be necessary but not sufficient. Completeness and immediacy of the output may matter more than trust in the source.
