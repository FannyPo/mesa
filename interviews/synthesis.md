## Interview 1: Tere
**Situation:** Pre-diabetic, working adult, primary caregiver for children. Identified in notes as Sister 1.

**Key quotes:**
- "Honestly, I didn't really 'decide.' I ate the same thing I usually eat — hard-boiled egg and a couple of quinoa crackers and coffee in the morning, skipped lunch because work was crazy, and then made rice, beans, and chicken for dinner because it's what the kids will eat. I know the rice isn't great for my sugar, but I was too tired to think of something else."
- "All the time. I buy vegetables with good intentions, but by the time I get around to cooking, they're already soft or moldy. I feel guilty throwing them away, but it happens every week."
- "I went after work, already exhausted. I didn't have a list. I just walked around grabbing things I usually buy. I looked at some healthier stuff, but it felt expensive, and I didn't know what meals to make with it, so I put it back hoping I can plan it better next week, but I still buy eggs and quinoa crackers… lol"
- "Not really. I usually just watch YouTube recipes, but they use ingredients I don't have, and TikTok videos because the videos are shorter."
- "Honestly? I just don't eat or I repeat the same meal again. I have the intention, but not a good system."

**What surprised me or challenged my assumptions:**
- She knows rice is not good for her blood sugar and chooses it anyway — not from ignorance but because it is the only thing the kids will eat. The constraint is not knowledge or even planning; it is that the family's food preferences and her dietary needs are in direct conflict. A blood-sugar-safe plan still has to feed children who do not have blood sugar concerns. This was not in the original problem framing.
- Produce waste produces guilt, not just financial frustration. She described it as something that "happens every week" — meaning it is already accepted as inevitable, not experienced as a solvable problem. That changes the pitch: she may not be looking for a solution to waste because she does not believe one exists.

## Interview 2: Mary
**Situation:** Pre-diabetic, working adult, family caregiver. Identified in notes as Sister 2.

**Key quotes:**
- "I had oatmeal for breakfast because it's 'healthy,' even though I'm not sure about the sugar. Lunch was fast food because I was running errands. Dinner was whatever I could make quickly — Mac and Cheese and a chicken from Publix. Sometimes I do salads too."
- "Yes. Especially spinach and berries. They go bad so fast. I buy them because I know I should eat healthier, but I never plan meals around them."
- "I went on Sunday. I tried to buy healthier things, but I didn't know what meals to make. I ended up buying the same stuff I always buy. I spent more than I wanted to."
- "I tried a low carb cookbook, but the recipes were too complicated. I don't have time for all that prep."
- "TikTok. But those recipes look good and then I never make them. I think I have all the ingredients and then I'm always missing a couple of them."

**What surprised me or challenged my assumptions:**
- She is not sure whether oatmeal is safe for blood sugar. The original problem statement says "the problem is not a lack of nutritional knowledge." Mary's answer challenges that directly. She is operating on incomplete and possibly incorrect information about which foods affect blood sugar. The system gap is real, but the knowledge gap may also be real and running underneath it.
- She overspends even when she is not buying healthier food. The assumption was that budget pressure comes from the cost of healthy eating. Her grocery trip suggests the bigger driver is the absence of a plan — without a list or a meal structure, she spends more regardless of what is in the cart.

## Interview 3: Grace
**Situation:** Pre-diabetic or high risk, budget-constrained. Identified in notes as Friend 1.

**Key quotes:**
- "I ate whatever was cheap. Eggs for breakfast, ramen for lunch, and a frozen meal for dinner. I know ramen is bad for sugar, but it's cheap and fast — and then I try to go walking… hahah"
- "Yes. I buy veggies thinking I'll cook, but then I'm too tired after work. They sit in the fridge until they're gross, or I cook them all at once to eat over a couple of days and they get soggy."
- "I went with a strict budget — $60. I bought basics: eggs, bread, chicken, rice. I wanted to buy healthier stuff, but it didn't fit the budget. I did buy mixed frozen vegetables."
- "I tried a diabetes app once, but it felt like it was made for older people. Not helpful."
- "Google. But it gives too many answers and I don't know which one to look at. I feel like I spend a lot of time."

**What surprised me or challenged my assumptions:**
- She tried batch cooking as a workaround — cooking all the vegetables at once to get through them — and it failed because they got soggy over multiple days. She is already attempting to solve the perishability problem herself, and her solution does not work. The system needs to account for texture and palatability across days, not just planning. Ingredient-first planning is not enough if the resulting meals are unpleasant by day three.
- Her grocery budget is a hard number: $60. Not a preference, a ceiling. Any system that increases the upfront grocery bill — even to save money over time — will not work for her. The value proposition has to pay off within the same week, not over a month.
- The diabetes app felt "made for older people." Cultural and demographic fit matters in how a tool is perceived and whether it gets used. This is not a feature problem. It is a design and positioning problem.

## Interview 4: Nelsy
**Situation:** High blood pressure with dietary restrictions (low sodium), no professional support. Identified in notes as Friend 2. Not pre-diabetic.

**Key quotes:**
- "I try to avoid salt, but it's hard. I ate cereal for breakfast, a sandwich for lunch, and baked chicken for dinner. I decide based on what's fast."
- "Yes. Especially things like zucchini or herbs. I buy them for one recipe and then don't use the rest."
- "I walked around and bought the main things we always eat. I know I need low sodium options, but I don't know which ones. I read labels, but it takes forever."
- "I tried a meal kit once, but it was too expensive. I canceled after two weeks."
- "I call mom and she gives me these recipes. They are healthy, but I don't end up cooking them."

**What surprised me or challenged my assumptions:**
- Nelsy is not pre-diabetic. She has high blood pressure and low-sodium restrictions. She was included in the interview set, but her condition and dietary constraints are different from the target customer. Her data confirms the structural problem (waste, no plan, price sensitivity) but it does not validate the blood sugar logic specifically. Five interviews is a small sample. One of five is outside the target diagnosis.
- She trusts her mother's recipes — described as healthy — and still does not cook them. The gap is not the source of advice, the credibility of it, or the quality of the recipes. She has good information from a trusted source and it does not convert to action. This is a meaningful challenge to any solution that assumes building trust will drive behavior change. Trust alone is not enough.
- Label reading takes forever. She knows she needs low-sodium options and cannot identify them efficiently in the aisle. This is friction that occurs before any planning or cooking happens. It suggests the problem starts at the store, not in the kitchen.

## Interview 5: Veronica
**Situation:** Pre-diabetic or high risk. Identified in notes as Friend 3.

**Key quotes:**
- "I had yogurt for breakfast, skipped lunch, and ordered takeout for dinner. I didn't plan anything. I just went with whatever was easiest."
- "Yes. I buy fruit and veggies, but I forget they're there. I don't know how to use them before they spoil. I don't like vegetables. I know I need to eat them though."
- "I walked in with an idea of what I should buy, and I bought things that 'seemed healthy,' but I didn't know what meals to make. I spent too much."
- "I tried a free meal prep app, but it felt like homework. Too many notifications."
- "Pinterest. But I never actually cook the recipes."

**What surprised me or challenged my assumptions:**
- "I don't like vegetables. I know I need to eat them though." This is the only interviewee who said she does not like the food the solution would be built around. A planning system cannot fix palatability. If the meals it generates are built on vegetables she actively dislikes, she will not follow the plan regardless of how well-designed it is. The problem for her may not be a system gap — it may be a behavior and preference problem that a food system cannot solve alone.
- Produce waste for her is driven by forgetting, not by fatigue or schedule. The food is in the refrigerator and she does not think about it. This is a different failure mode than Tere's exhaustion or Grace's batch cooking. Not all five interviewees are failing for the same reason, which matters if the solution is built around one mechanism.
- A free app still got abandoned — not because of cost but because it felt like homework and had too many notifications. Price is not the only adoption barrier. Engagement mechanics that require ongoing input create friction that overrides even zero cost.

## Cross-Interview Patterns

**Consistent across all five:**
- Produce waste happens every week without exception.
- Grocery shopping occurs without a list or meal plan.
- All five tried at least one tool or resource (YouTube, TikTok, Pinterest, a cookbook, an app, a meal kit) and stopped using it.
- Inspiration — from social media, recipes, or family advice — does not convert into meals. Tere: TikTok videos don't match her pantry. Mary: missing a couple of ingredients breaks the chain. Veronica: "Pinterest. But I never actually cook the recipes."
- The strongest confirmation across all five, in Tere's words: "I have the intention, but not a good system."

**Patterns that complicate the thesis:**
- The knowledge gap may be real alongside the system gap. Mary does not know whether oatmeal is safe for her blood sugar. This sits in tension with the claim that the problem is not nutritional knowledge. At minimum, both gaps appear to coexist.
- Not all five interviewees share the same root failure. Tere and Grace fail from exhaustion. Mary fails from ingredient mismatch. Nelsy fails from information overload at the label. Veronica fails from forgetting and dislike of vegetables. A single system built around one mechanism may not address all of them.
- One of five interviewees (Nelsy) is not the target customer by diagnosis. Her condition is different, her constraints are different, and her data is adjacent but not confirmatory for blood sugar logic.
- Family food preferences are a real constraint that was not in the original problem statement. Tere eats rice because the kids will eat it. The system has to work for a household, not just an individual.
- Trust does not drive behavior. Nelsy trusts her mother's healthy recipes. Tere and Mary trust TikTok enough to watch it. None of them act on it. Whatever is missing is not trust.
