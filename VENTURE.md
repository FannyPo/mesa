# VENTURE.md
Pre-Diabetic Family Weekly Food System

---

## The Problem

Pre-diabetic adults who work full time, attend school, and care for a family have no practical weekly food system that connects their grocery budget to realistic ingredient quantities, perishability, and schedule constraints. Without this system, food spoils before it is used, money is wasted twice, and they default to processed food that worsens their condition. The problem is not a lack of nutritional knowledge. It is the complete absence of a system designed for people managing blood sugar risk under real financial and time pressure. The gap is not storage of nutritional information or meal ideas — those exist everywhere. The gap is the interface layer: nothing pulls budget, perishability, schedule, and blood sugar logic together into one connected system. Every competitor solves one piece. The coordination layer between all four constraints is entirely missing.

---

## Target Customer

The pre-diabetic adult simultaneously working full time, attending school or continuing education, and serving as primary caregiver for a family — on a household income tight enough that grocery waste is felt immediately as a financial loss. They are 35 and older, diagnosed as pre-diabetic or at high risk of Type 2 diabetes progression, and have no access to a nutritionist, dietitian, or meal delivery service. They manage their condition largely alone through YouTube, Google, and Facebook groups. They know what they should eat. The failure is execution under real pressure: a full schedule, a limited budget, a family to feed, and no system to make it work.

Validated through five interviews. All five interviewees reported the same pattern: produce bought with good intentions spoils weekly, grocery trips happen without a list, social media (TikTok, YouTube, Pinterest) inspires but never converts into meals, and existing tools were tried and abandoned. The clearest articulation came from Tere: *"I have the intention, but not a good system."*

---

## Competitive Landscape

**PlateJoy (2013–2025)**
The closest competitor to this venture's target space. Built a personalized meal-planning platform using 50+ data points and launched a CDC-recognized Diabetes Prevention Program for pre-diabetic users with weight tracking and virtual coaching. Raised approximately $12.3 million. Failed for two compounding reasons: it pivoted from direct-to-consumer to a B2B insurance channel, making revenue dependent on user health outcomes it could not control, and HIPAA restrictions limited how it could communicate with users. Acquired by RVO Health in 2021; the founder left, development froze, and the product was shut down in 2025. The lesson: routing through insurance reimbursement made it inaccessible to the exact budget-constrained families who need it most.

**Klinio**
Targets a clinical or insurance channel rather than the direct consumer. Labels a general wellness product with a diabetes-friendly tag. Does not integrate budget, perishability, or schedule.

**eMeals and general meal planning apps**
Generic tools that are too rigid and not built around pre-diabetic constraints, bulk purchasing, or perishability. Subscription pricing at $5–15 per month before adding real grocery or delivery costs is structurally incompatible with the target customer — not inconvenient, a complete barrier to entry.

**Meal kits**
Tried and abandoned due to cost. Willingness to try exists; price is the barrier, not behavior.

**Where every competitor fails**
No product integrates all four constraints simultaneously — pre-diabetes blood sugar logic, a real grocery budget, ingredient perishability, and a family's weekly schedule — into a repeatable, automated system. Every competitor solves one or two. None connect all four. More critically, none account for the fundamental mismatch between how food is sold and how meals are planned: a recipe calls for green beans, the store sells a two-pound bag, the recipe uses half, the rest spoils, and no tool thinks across that gap.

## Direction Identified

**Leaning toward Remix 1 (complaint-driven, direct-to-consumer):** An AI-powered weekly food system that takes a family's budget, refrigerator inventory, local grocery sales, and weekly schedule and generates a fresh, blood-sugar-safe meal plan every week. Repetition is structurally impossible because every week's inputs are different.

The "why now" argument is clean: this system was impossible before AI because no prior technology could simultaneously reason across budget, perishability, schedule, and blood sugar safety. That capability is new, and it is the core reason this gap still exists and can now be closed.

Two leverage points identified from the systems analysis:
- **Ingredient-first planning:** Plan three meals around one bag of green beans before buying. Breaks the waste loop at the source.
- **Built-in variety engine:** Same ingredient, different preparation each day. Breaks the fatigue loop and prevents abandonment.

**Remix 2** (B2B through community health clinics or employers) and **Remix 3** (grocery chain partnership funded by predictable weekly purchases) remain viable as future business model layers once the core product is validated.

**Open questions interviews still need to confirm:**
- Do pre-diabetic parents describe their problem as a system breakdown, not a knowledge gap?
- Would they trust AI for a medically sensitive condition — and what would need to be true for that trust?
- What is the maximum realistic monthly payment, and does it support a sustainable business?

## Revenue Model

**Chosen model:** Grocery chain partnership. A regional grocery chain pays a per-user licensing fee for access to pre-diabetic shoppers who plan their weekly meals around the chain's sales circular. The end user pays nothing. Working price estimate: $5/active user/month, derived from comparable loyalty engagement tools and positioned below PlateJoy's healthcare channel rate of $30–50/member/month. This number has no real-world validation — no chain has been approached.

**Unit economics:** At $5/user/month, variable costs run $0.60/user (AI inference, hosting, and customer support), producing a 64% gross margin per user once hosting and data integration are allocated to cost of goods. Fixed operating costs — clinical advisor at 20 hours/month, insurance, compliance, and data integration maintenance — total $4,500/month. Break-even requires approximately 1,023 active users from a single chain deal. At 0.5% activation of a 50,000-member loyalty base (250 users), the model runs a $4,000/month operating deficit indefinitely. At 2% activation (1,000 users) with the chain actively promoting the tool, the model approaches break-even. The difference between those two outcomes is almost entirely determined by whether the chain treats the tool as a priority or a background feature — a negotiation point, not a product decision.

**Biggest financial risk:** The churn rate. All five interviewees abandoned every tool they tried — a free app, a meal kit, a cookbook, a diabetes app, TikTok recipes, and a trusted family member's personalized advice. The base financial scenario assumes 20% monthly churn on the premise that embedding the tool in the weekly grocery context changes the abandonment pattern. That premise is unproven. If churn runs at 40% — which the interview data actually supports — the user base decays from 250 to 11 active users within six months of launch, revenue falls to $55/month against $4,500/month in fixed costs, and the model never recovers without constant chain-funded re-acquisition campaigns. The venture's financial viability depends entirely on solving the abandonment problem that every prior tool in this category failed to solve.

**What the pricing test must resolve:** The Wizard of Oz test running this week sends a manually built, store-specific weekly plan to Tere, Mary, and Grace before their next grocery trip. It is testing whether a complete, ready-to-execute output actually changes what they buy — not whether they find it interesting, but whether they use it. If at least two of the three change their cart based on the plan and one asks for another one next week, the 20% churn assumption in the base scenario has behavioral support and the venture has a viable path. If none of them follow the plan — if they read it, call it helpful, and buy the same cart they always buy — the product replicates the Nelsy pattern: trusted, personalized, ready-to-use information that still does not bridge to action. In that case, the churn problem is not solvable through better planning delivery, and the revenue model requires a fundamentally different product intervention before any financial projection is worth building.
