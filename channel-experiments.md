## Channel 1 — Instagram

**Status:** Not yet run
**URL:** mesa-bm3.pages.dev/?ref=ig
**Tracking:** Cloudflare analytics, filter by ref=ig

---

### Why This Channel

All five interviewees are active on Instagram and post Stories. Fanny has 476 followers and has never posted — the first post creates a genuine surprise/curiosity signal that a regular account cannot manufacture. Most importantly, the WoZ households (Tere, Mary, Grace, and now Susana and Cindy) already follow Fanny. This is not a cold audience: the people who have already used Mesa and loved it will see this post and can publicly signal their experience in the comments. That social proof is the asset.

---

### Experiment Design

Two-part post: a **feed post** for comments and public social proof, plus an **Instagram Story with a link sticker** immediately after, because feed captions cannot hold clickable links.

---

### Part 1 — Feed Post

**Content choice (pick one):**
- **Option A — Susana's Aldi plan:** A photo or clean screenshot of the full 7-day Aldi low-carb Dominican meal plan you built for Susana. One image or a 2–3 slide carousel showing the grocery list and the week's meals. Visual is real output, not a mockup.
- **Option B — Cindy/Susana side-by-side:** The comparison of both plans shown together. More visually compelling if the two plans look meaningfully different — shows that Mesa personalizes to each family, not a generic template.

Choose whichever looks more complete and real on screen. Real beats polished.

**Caption:**

```
Hi guys, I know I never post 😅 But I've been building something.

A lot of us buy vegetables with good intentions every week. They go 
soft in the back of the fridge every week. We go to the store without 
a list, look at the healthier stuff, and put it back because we don't 
know what meals to make with it. We have the intention. We just don't 
have a system.

Last week I made a 7-day plan for one of the families I've been testing 
with, built around what was on sale at Aldi. She took it to the store 
and followed it.

If this sounds like you, the Story has the link, or it's in my bio.
```

**Why this caption works:**
- First line is the entire hook — the anomaly (years of silence) earns the tap before anything else loads
- "A lot of us" makes the reader the subject immediately — they're not observing someone else's problem, they're in it
- The vegetable waste and listless shopping are behaviors, not health claims — anyone who has done a grocery run recognizes this
- "We have the intention. We just don't have a system." — Tere's idea, folded into the collective voice, no attribution needed
- One specific proof: a real family, a real store, a real outcome — not a concept
- No product name, no health condition language, no sign-up ask in the feed
- "Story has the link, or it's in my bio" — CTA lives in the Story where clicks actually happen

**Posting notes:**
- Post between 7–9 PM on a weekday, or Sunday morning — highest Instagram engagement windows
- Do not add hashtags on the first post; they can flag new accounts as spam
- Do not tag Mesa's account if one exists — keep this personal
- Let your WoZ households (Tere, Mary, Grace, Susana, Cindy) know the post is going up before you publish, so they don't miss the 24-hour window. Their comments are their choice — don't script them, just give them the heads-up.

---

### Part 2 — Story (Post Immediately After the Feed Post)

Post this within 5 minutes of the feed post going live, while it is still at the top of followers' feeds.

**Slide 1:**
- Screenshot of the feed post you just published, cropped to show the image and first two lines of the caption
- Add a text sticker: "just posted ↓"

**Slide 2:**
- Plain background (use Mesa's olive green or cream if you have it from the brand guide)
- Large text: "Want a plan for your family?"
- Sub-text: "Early access — free while we're testing"
- Link sticker pointing to: **mesa-bm3.pages.dev/?ref=ig**
- Small text below the link: "Takes 2 minutes"

**Why Story + feed:**
Stories are where your followers go first. The link sticker on a Story is the only clickable link available to you on Instagram without a business account. The feed post builds social proof over time (comments accumulate, it stays visible). The Story captures clicks in the first 24 hours while the audience is warm.

---

### Success Criteria

| Signal | Threshold | What It Means |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| Link clicks (Cloudflare, ref=ig) | **8+** | Warm audience is interested enough to leave Instagram |
| Sign-ups from those clicks | **1+** | Interest converts to intent |

**Success:** 8+ link clicks AND 1+ sign-up → Instagram is a viable acquisition channel. Scale with one post per week.

**Failure signal:** Under 3 clicks AND zero engagement (no comments, no DMs) → the warm audience did not respond. Before concluding the channel failed, check whether the feed post is visible on your profile and whether the Story link sticker posted correctly — a technical failure is not a content failure.

---

### What to Watch For

**Primary signals:**
- **WoZ comments:** If Tere, Mary, Grace, Susana, or Cindy comment publicly, screenshot it — that is social proof you did not have to manufacture, and it changes how every subsequent reader weighs the post.
- **DMs:** People who are genuinely interested often DM instead of clicking. "How does this work?" is a warmer signal than a link click from someone who bounced in 10 seconds. Count DMs as leads even if they don't show up in Cloudflare.

**Secondary signals:**
- **Comments from outside your close circle:** A comment from someone who is not a WoZ household or close friend means the content resonated with a warm stranger. That is the reach signal — more meaningful than a like.
- **Story views vs. link taps:** Check Instagram's Story analytics after 24 hours. If you got 40 Story views and 2 link taps, the content is working but the CTA is not landing. If you got 5 Story views total, the feed post did not drive anyone to your Stories — the sequencing broke down.
- **Time-to-first-click:** Check Cloudflare within 2 hours of posting. Zero clicks by hour 2 usually means the Story link sticker is broken or did not post correctly.
- **Zero engagement, zero clicks:** Check that the feed post is publicly visible on your profile before drawing conclusions — first posts from quiet accounts are occasionally held briefly.

---

### Run Window

Post within this week. Story expires in 24 hours — the link click window closes with it. The feed post stays up and continues to collect comments, but the majority of link clicks will come from the Story in the first 24 hours.

## Channel 2 — WhatsApp (Individual DMs)

**Status:** Not yet run
**URL:** mesa-bm3.pages.dev/?ref=wa
**Tracking:** Cloudflare analytics (ref=wa total clicks) + sign-up names as the forward indicator
**Run window:** 3–4 days after Channel 1 (Instagram) posts — let the Story window close and results settle before sending

---

### Why This Channel

Fanny messages family individually, not in group chats. She picks 3–5 family members and sends each a personal one-on-one message. This experiment does not measure Fanny's reach — she is only texting 3–5 people. It measures the **forward**: whether any of those 5 pass Mesa to someone else, someone Fanny has never met. That is Mesa's natural growth engine.

The measurement is clean: Fanny knows exactly who she texted. Any sign-up name she does not recognize = confirmed forward.

---

### Step 1 — Pick the Right 3–5 People

Not every family member qualifies. Each person you pick should clear both filters:

**Filter 1 — They cook.** They are the one in their household who decides what to eat, shops, and cooks. If they don't cook, they have no one to forward this to.

**Filter 2 — They forward things.** They are the person in the family who sends health articles, recipes, and tips to other people. Every family has one. If they got a good meal plan, they would send it to their sister, their coworker, their neighbor. Pick that person.

Write the list before you send anything. The list is part of the experiment — you need to know exactly who you messaged so you can identify an unfamiliar sign-up name as a forward.

---

### Step 2 — What to Attach

Send the Susana plan (or Cindy/Susana side-by-side) as an image in the same message — image first, text second. The image is what gets forwarded. If the plan is a PDF or document, screenshot the most readable page (the weekly meal table or the grocery list) and send that. A clean screenshot travels better than a file attachment.

---

### Step 3 — The Message (Spanish/Spanglish — adjust naturally for each person)

```
Oye, he estado working on something y quería compartírtelo antes de 
que lo abra a más gente.

Es un sistema que planea la comida de la semana según tu presupuesto 
y lo que está en sale en tu súper, pa' que no se desperdicie nada en 
el fridge. La semana pasada lo probé con una familia y ella lo llevó 
al súper y lo siguió.

Si conoces a alguien que lo necesite, mándaselo.
Y si tú lo quieres también, me dices. 🙋

mesa-bm3.pages.dev/?ref=wa
```

**Why this copy works:**
- "antes de que lo abra a más gente" signals early access — she is getting it before it goes wider, which is true and earns attention
- "working on something" in Spanglish is the natural register — personal, not promotional
- No health conditions, no product name, no startup framing
- "mándaselo" is the forward ask, placed before the self-ask — lead with the forward
- "si tú lo quieres también, me dices" is the pull for the "can you make one for me?" reply
- Link at the end, reads like an afterthought not a sales pitch

**You can adjust** "pa' que no se desperdicie nada" to however you'd naturally say it — "pa' que no se dañe nada," "pa' que no se bote nada" — the phrase is yours to own. Do not add health condition language.

---

### How the Forward Is Measured

Fanny knows the names of the 3–5 people she texted. Every sign-up on mesa-bm3.pages.dev that shows a name she does not recognize = someone forwarded it.

Cloudflare shows total ref=wa clicks but cannot distinguish direct from forwarded clicks. That is fine — the sign-up names do the work.

| What you see | What it means |
|---|---|
| Sign-up name you recognize | They signed up themselves — interest, not a forward |
| Sign-up name you don't recognize | Confirmed forward — Mesa traveled on its own |
| Reply: "me haces uno?" or equivalent | Word-of-mouth pull — someone wants a plan |
| ref=wa clicks but zero sign-ups | Interest without conversion — landing page may be the issue |

After 48 hours, ask each person you texted directly: "¿Se lo mandaste a alguien?" This surfaces forwards that happened without a link click — someone sent the image to another chat without touching the URL.

---

### Success Criteria

| Signal | Threshold | What It Means |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| Sign-up from someone Fanny did NOT text | **1+** | Mesa traveled — word-of-mouth is the engine |
| Reply: "can you make one for me?" or equivalent | **1+** | Pull signal — the product sells itself in conversation |

**Success:** Either of the above → word-of-mouth is a real growth channel. Design for it explicitly in the product — every plan should be easy to screenshot and forward.

**Failure signal:** No forwards, no replies after 48 hours. Before concluding the channel failed: check whether messages were read (WhatsApp shows individual read receipts), then ask each person directly if they shared it. Silence plus no read receipt = bad timing. Silence plus read receipt = the message did not move them.

---

### Timing

Send Thursday or Friday — people think about grocery shopping heading into the weekend. Send in the afternoon, not the morning. Morning texts get buried.

---

### Why This Channel

Channels 1 and 2 tested warm audiences — people who know Fanny or are one degree away. This is the first cold test: local strangers with no relationship to Fanny, no prior knowledge of Mesa, and no reason to engage except that the content is useful to them. That is what makes it the real market test. If cold strangers in the same geography and income bracket as the target customer engage with a neighborhood post, the product has pull beyond Fanny's personal network.

Casselberry is the right geography — a working-class suburb in Seminole County, the same area as the target customer. These people shop at the same stores, face the same grocery budgets, and are not pre-selected for health consciousness. If it lands here, it lands on the real market.

---

### Framing Rule

**Use the grocery/budget angle. Do not open with the health-condition angle.**

"A weekly meal plan built around whatever's on sale" is for everyone in Casselberry. "Pre-diabetic meal planning" immediately narrows the audience to people who have already received a diagnosis and are in active management mode — most readers will self-select out before they know whether the product is for them. Lead with the universal problem (spending more than you meant to at the grocery store and still not knowing what to cook), and the people who need the blood sugar logic will find it on the landing page.

---

### Post Structure

**Put the link in the first comment, not the caption.** Facebook's algorithm suppresses posts with external links in the body. Post the image and caption first, then immediately add the first comment with the link. This keeps the post visible while still directing people to the page. The caption ends with "link in my first comment" so readers know where to look.

---

### What to Attach

Post the Susana plan screenshot (or Cindy/Susana side-by-side) as the image. The image is what earns credibility — it shows a real output, not a concept. Make sure the screenshot is readable at mobile size (the weekly meal table should be legible without zooming). If the image has any visible health-condition language in the header, crop it out before posting — keep the image on the grocery/meal angle.

---

### The Post (copy this, adjust naturally)

**Caption:**

```
I've been building a free meal planning tool and wanted to share it 
with the neighborhood before it goes wider.

You tell it your grocery budget and what's on sale at your store that 
week. It builds a full 7-day plan — what to buy, what to cook, in what 
order so nothing spoils before you use it.

This is what it made for a friend last week based on Aldi's sales. She 
took the list straight to the store and followed it. First week in a 
long time she didn't throw anything away.

It's free right now — I'm still testing it and want real feedback from 
real people. Link's in my first comment. Happy to answer any questions 
here. 👇
```

**First comment (post immediately after, within 30 seconds):**

```
👉 mesa-bm3.pages.dev/?ref=fb
```

**Why this post works:**

- "before it goes wider" signals that Casselberry is getting early access — exclusivity without a pitch
- "you tell it your budget and what's on sale" — the mechanism is immediately understandable in one sentence, no jargon
- "nothing spoils before you use it" — names the produce waste pain point without calling it a pain point; every Casselberry household knows this feeling
- "she didn't throw anything away" — the concrete result, in plain language, from a real person
- "I'm still testing it and want real feedback" — removes the promotional smell; a neighbor testing something is not a business running an ad
- "Happy to answer any questions here" — invites comments, which boost algorithmic reach, and keeps the conversation in the post where others can see it

**Do not** mention Mesa by name, describe it as a startup, use any health condition language, or include a call-to-action that sounds like an ad ("Sign up today," "Limited spots available"). The moment it reads like marketing, a moderator removes it.

---

### If the Group Has a Monday Post Format

Some neighborhood Facebook groups require Monday business posts to follow a specific format (a header, a description of your business, etc.). Check the group pinned post or rules before writing the final caption. If a format is required, follow it — but keep the neighbor tone inside whatever structure is required. A required format is not a license to write ad copy.

---

### Success Criteria

| Signal | Threshold | What It Means |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| Link clicks (Cloudflare, ref=fb) | **5+** | Cold strangers are interested enough to leave Facebook |
| Sign-up from those clicks | **1+** | Cold interest converts to intent |
| Comment: "can you share the template?" or equivalent | **1+** | Pull from someone who didn't even click — strong signal |

**Success:** 5+ link clicks AND (1+ sign-up OR 1+ template request comment) → cold local strangers respond to the budget/grocery framing. The neighborhood channel is viable.

**Failure signal:** Zero engagement (no likes, no comments, no clicks) OR the post is removed by a moderator. Zero engagement on a Monday post in an active neighborhood group means the content did not earn attention from cold readers — the framing or the image failed. A removed post means it read as an ad regardless of intent; adjust the tone and try the next Monday.

---

### What to Watch For

- **Comments before clicks:** In Facebook groups, people often comment before they click a link — "What store does it work with?" or "Is this free?" Answer every comment directly and publicly. Each response is visible to everyone else reading the post and extends its reach.
- **Post removed within the hour:** Read the group rules again. Either business posts are not actually permitted on this Monday or the post read as promotional to a moderator. Wait one week, adjust the framing, try again.
- **"I need this" comments with no click:** They responded emotionally but didn't go further. Reply with the link directly in the comment thread: "Here you go — it's free right now: [link]." Make the next step zero effort.
- **Check Cloudflare at 2 hours and again at 24 hours.** Facebook post reach peaks in the first few hours for group posts. If you have zero clicks at hour 2, the post is not being seen — check whether it posted successfully and whether it's visible in the group feed.
